29. Fungi, Protists & Disease

Key Notes (English)

Adapted from the provided speech. Focus: what fungi and protists are, examples of their diseases (rose black spot and malaria), and how these diseases spread and can be controlled.

1) What Are Fungi?
  • Fungi are eukaryotic organisms, just like plants and animals.
  • They can be unicellular (single-celled) or multicellular.
  • Yeast is an example of a unicellular fungus used to make bread rise.
  • Mushrooms are examples of multicellular fungi.
2) Hyphae and Spores
  • Many multicellular fungi have long, thread-like structures called hyphae.
  • Hyphae grow out from the main body of the fungus and can spread through the soil.
  • Sometimes hyphae spread over plant surfaces or even penetrate human skin, causing disease.
  • Hyphae can produce spores, which spread easily and grow into new fungi.
3) Rose Black Spot – Symptoms and Effects
  • Fungal disease Rose black spot is a disease of plants, especially roses.
  • It causes purple or black spots to form on the leaves.
  • As the fungus damages the plant further, the leaves may turn yellow and eventually drop off.
  • This reduces the plant's ability to photosynthesise, so it does not grow well.
4) Spread and Treatment of Rose Black Spot
  • The rose black spot fungus is usually spread by water or by the wind.
  • One treatment is to cut off all infected leaves.
  • Another treatment is to spray the plant with fungicides, which kill the fungus.
  • Infected leaves that are removed should be destroyed, so the fungus cannot spread to other plants.
5) What Are Protists?
  • Like fungi, protists are also eukaryotes.
  • They can be single-celled or multicellular, but most are unicellular.
  • Some protists are parasites, living on or inside other organisms.
  • Parasites survive at the host's expense, often causing harm to the host.
6) Vectors and Protist Diseases
  • Protist diseases are often spread by vectors.
  • A vector is another organism, such as an insect, that transports the protist between hosts.
  • Importantly, the vector does not usually get the disease itself.
  • The vector simply carries the protist from one host to another.
7) Malaria – Cause and Transmission
  • Protist disease Malaria is caused by a parasitic protist.
  • The protist needs a host, such as a human or other animal, in order to survive.
  • Malaria is transported between hosts by mosquitoes.
  • When a mosquito feeds on an infected animal, it can suck up some of the malaria parasites in the blood.
  • Later, when the mosquito feeds on a healthy person, the parasites are transferred to the new host.
8) Malaria – Symptoms and Dangers
  • When humans catch malaria, they can develop severe fevers and bad headaches.
  • People with malaria generally feel very unwell and dreadful.
  • The symptoms often go away for a time but then keep coming back.
  • These repeating attacks are called recurrent episodes.
  • In some cases, malaria can be fatal.
9) Preventing Malaria – Controlling Vectors
  • The best way to prevent malaria is to stop the vectors from spreading the protist.
  • We can reduce mosquito numbers by destroying their breeding sites.
  • Mosquitoes can also be killed directly using insecticides.
10) Preventing Mosquito Bites
  • Another strategy is to stop mosquitoes feeding on humans.
  • People can sleep under mosquito nets to avoid bites at night.
  • Mosquito repellents can also be used to keep mosquitoes away from the skin.
  • By reducing bites, we reduce the spread of malaria between human hosts.
