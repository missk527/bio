Notes (Key points from the speech)

What is diffusion?
  • Definition: Diffusion is the net movement of particles from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration.
  • It occurs because particles move randomly. They do not “aim” for low concentration; spreading out is the overall result of chance movement.
  • When many particles start bunched together, they gradually spread out until they are roughly evenly distributed in the available space.
  • “Net movement” means considering the group overall: individual particles can move either way at any moment, but overall flow is from high → low concentration.
Where does diffusion happen?
  • In gases (e.g., a sprayed perfume smell moving across a whole room).
  • In liquids/solutions (e.g., food colouring dispersing in a beaker until the colour is uniform).
  • Across partially permeable membranes such as cell membranes.
Diffusion through cell membranes
  • Cell membranes are partially permeable: only some molecules can cross by diffusion.
  • Typically, small dissolved molecules diffuse through: water, glucose, amino acids (also gases like oxygen and carbon dioxide).
  • Large molecules (e.g., proteins, starch) are too big to pass by simple diffusion.
  • Diffusion is a passive process—no energy from the cell is required.
Factors affecting the rate of diffusion
  • Concentration gradient: bigger difference in concentration → faster diffusion.
  • Temperature: higher temperature gives particles more kinetic energy → faster random motion → faster diffusion.
  • Surface area: larger membrane surface area → more particles can cross at once → faster diffusion.
