Notes (Key points from the speech)

What monoclonal antibodies are
  • Monoclonal antibodies are antibodies made from a single clone of cells.
  • A single antibody‑producing cell is cloned many times, and the clones produce identical antibodies that can be collected and purified.
Antibodies, B cells, and antigens
  • Antibodies are proteins made by white blood cells called B lymphocytes (B cells).
  • They help fight disease by binding to antigens—foreign molecules (e.g., on a bacterium’s cell wall).
  • Each antibody has a specific binding site, so it binds only to antigens with the matching shape.
Why we use hybridoma cells
  • To make antibodies on a large scale in the lab, we need many B‑cell clones, but normal B cells do not divide quickly.
  • Scientists fuse antibody‑producing B cells with fast‑dividing tumour cells.
  • The fused cell is called a hybridoma (a hybrid cell): it divides rapidly like a tumour cell and makes antibodies like a B cell.
  • Hybridomas are grown in a petri dish to produce lots of identical antibody‑producing cells, then the antibodies are collected and purified.
How we obtain the correct B cells
  • To get B cells that make the “right” antibody, an animal (often a mouse) is injected with the target antigen.
  • The animal’s immune system produces many B cells that are specific to that antigen.
  • Those B cells can then be isolated and fused with tumour cells to create hybridomas.
Why monoclonal antibodies are useful
  • Monoclonal antibodies bind to one specific target.
  • By choosing the right B cell, we can make antibodies that target a particular protein, cell type, pathogen, or chemical.
  • They can also be “loaded” with substances such as drugs, fluorescent markers, or radioactive material.
Example: targeting cancer cells
  • If a cancer cell has a unique antigen, monoclonal antibodies can be designed to bind to it.
  • Attaching a drug or radioactive material allows the antibody to deliver treatment directly to the cancer cells, helping to locate and destroy them.
