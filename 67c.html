Pregnancy Tests — Monoclonal Antibodies hCG · Binding · Control Line

Video + exam‑focused notes + 40‑question practice

Notes (Key points from the speech)

What pregnancy tests detect
  • Pregnancy tests are cheap, quick, and reliable ways to check if a woman is pregnant.
  • Pregnant women produce a hormone called hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin).
  • hCG is excreted in urine, and the test detects whether hCG is present.
Monoclonal antibodies and specificity
  • The test uses monoclonal antibodies that are specific to hCG.
  • “Specific” means the antibodies can bind to hCG if they meet it.
Two key parts of the test strip
  • Fixed antibodies zone: antibodies attached to the strip that cannot move.
  • Mobile blue beads zone: blue beads coated with the same anti-hCG antibodies; these beads are free to move with urine.
Negative result (not pregnant)
  • If there is no hCG, urine carries the mobile beads along the strip.
  • The beads pass over the fixed antibodies but do not stick (nothing to bind to).
  • No trapped beads → no coloured line (no blue strip).
Positive result (pregnant)
  • If hCG is present, it binds to antibodies on the blue beads first.
  • As the beads move, the hCG on the beads also binds to the fixed antibodies.
  • This forms a “sandwich” and the beads get trapped in the test region.
  • Trapping many beads creates a visible coloured line/strip that indicates a positive result.
Control line
  • Many real pregnancy tests show two lines rather than one.
  • The second line acts as a control to show the test has worked properly.
  • Typically, two lines = positive, but different tests vary, so always read the instructions.
Take-home message
  • Pregnancy tests detect hCG in urine using antibodies that bind specifically to hCG.
  • A coloured line appears when antibody-coated beads are trapped by fixed antibodies because hCG is present.
