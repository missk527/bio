Levels of Organization: Cells → Tissues → Organs → Systems

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

Overview
  • Humans are complex multicellular organisms. A helpful way to study this complexity is to view it in layers.
  • The typical progression is: cells → tissues → organs → organ systems → organism.
  • Cells are the basic building blocks of life and contain subcellular structures (e.g., nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes).
  • Most human cells are specialized for particular jobs (e.g., nerve cells, muscle cells).
Cells → Tissues
  • A tissue is a group of similar cells working together to perform a specific function.
  • Examples: muscle tissue (from muscle cells); epithelial tissue (from epithelial cells) that lines organs such as the lungs and intestines.
  • Plants follow the same layered idea: xylem cells form xylem, which transports water up the stem.
Tissues → Organs
  • Different types of tissues combine to form an organ.
  • Common examples: heart, stomach, pancreas, brain, kidneys.
  • Less obvious but still organs: teeth, tendons, bronchi, and salivary glands.
Organs → Organ Systems
  • Groups of organs that cooperate for a broad function make up an organ system.
  • Examples: cardiovascular system, digestive system, muscular system (among others).
  • Taken together, organ systems form the entire organism.
