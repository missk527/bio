58. Biotic & Abiotic Factors

Key Notes (English)

Adapted from the provided speech. Focus: definitions of biotic and abiotic factors, examples, and how they affect organisms using the clownfish ecosystem.

1) Biotic vs Abiotic Factors – Overview
  • Biotic factors are living factors that affect organisms or shape an ecosystem.
  • Abiotic factors are non-living parts of the environment that affect organisms.
  • Both types of factors interact to determine which species can survive and how large their populations are.
2) Biotic Factors – Definition & Idea
  • Biotic factors involve living organisms and their interactions.
  • They include any living process that can influence another organism.
  • Examples include:
    • Predation – one organism eating another.
    • Competition – organisms fighting or competing for resources or habitat.
    • Disease – caused by pathogens that are living organisms (bacteria, viruses, fungi, etc.).
    • Food availability – how much food is provided by other living organisms.
  • When you look for biotic factors, think of all the ways animals, plants, bacteria and other living things affect each other.
3) Abiotic Factors – Definition & Idea
  • Abiotic factors are the non-living, usually physical or chemical, parts of the environment.
  • They can still have a big effect on organisms and whole ecosystems.
  • Common abiotic factors include:
    • Light intensity
    • Temperature
    • Carbon dioxide (CO₂) concentration
    • Moisture levels
    • Wind intensity and direction
    • Soil pH and mineral content
4) Example: Changing Temperature
  • Abiotic factors can influence biological processes.
  • For example, increasing temperature can:
    • Increase the rate of photosynthesis because enzymes work faster (up to an optimum).
    • Lead to a higher overall rate of plant growth.
  • For animals, a warmer temperature may mean they spend less energy keeping warm.
  • They could then use more energy for growth or spend less time searching for food.
5) Typical Exam Questions
  • Questions often ask you to:
    • Identify biotic and abiotic factors in a given ecosystem.
    • Explain what might happen if one of these factors changes.
  • Always think about how a change in one factor can affect populations and interactions in the ecosystem.
6) Clownfish Example – Biotic Factors
  • The video uses a clownfish living near a sea anemone as an example.
  • Important biotic factors for the clownfish include:
    • Predation by bigger fish or eels.
    • The sea anemone itself, which acts as the clownfish’s habitat and protection.
    • Competition for the sea anemone (habitat) with other clownfish or species.
    • Competition for food such as plankton and algae.
    • The overall burden of disease affecting the fish.
7) Clownfish Example – Abiotic Factors
  • Important abiotic factors for the clownfish include:
    • Water temperature – can change during the day or across seasons.
    • Oxygen concentration in the water.
    • Acidity (pH) of the water.
    • Salt (salinity) levels in the water.
  • These non-living conditions will affect how well the clownfish and other organisms can survive.
8) Indirect Effects of Abiotic Factors
  • Some abiotic factors may not affect the clownfish directly, but can still influence it.
  • For example:
    • Light intensity and carbon dioxide concentration affect photosynthesis in nearby species such as the sea anemone or algae.
    • If these producers are affected, it will change the clownfish’s habitat and food supply.
  • So abiotic changes can cause indirect effects on organisms via other species in the food web.
9) How to Spot Biotic vs Abiotic in Any Ecosystem
  • To find biotic factors, list all the ways living things affect each other:
  • Who eats whom? Who competes for what? How does disease spread? What provides food or habitat?
  • To find abiotic factors, look for all the non-living conditions:
  • Light, temperature, water, gases, soil, wind, pH, minerals, etc.
  • Always decide if a factor is living (biotic) or non-living (abiotic).
10) Key Takeaways
  • Biotic = living factors and interactions (predation, competition, food, disease).
  • Abiotic = non-living environmental conditions (light, temperature, CO₂, water, soil, etc.).
  • Both types of factors work together to shape ecosystems and affect individual organisms like the clownfish.
