60. Food Chains & Predator–Prey Cycles

Key Notes (English)

Adapted from the provided speech. Focus: how energy flows through an ecosystem using food chains, and how predator–prey population cycles work.

1) Food Chains and Food Webs
  • A food chain shows what gets eaten by what in an ecosystem.
  • It is a simplified version of a food web, because it only shows one pathway of feeding.
  • Food webs are more complex and show many different feeding relationships in an ecosystem.
2) Producers – The Start of All Food Chains
  • All food chains start with a producer.
  • Producers are photosynthetic organisms, such as green plants or algae.
  • They can produce glucose by photosynthesis, using energy from the Sun.
  • We can use grass as a simple example of a producer in a food chain.
3) Biomass – Stored Energy in Organisms
  • When producers make biological molecules like sugars, these become part of the plant's biomass.
  • Biomass is the mass of living material and represents stored energy.
  • Energy from the Sun is therefore stored in the biomass of the producer and passed on when the producer is eaten.
4) Primary, Secondary and Tertiary Consumers
  • The next organisms in the food chain are the primary consumers.
  • Primary consumers eat the producers (for example, mice eating grass).
  • Secondary consumers feed on primary consumers and are often predators (such as an owl eating mice).
  • Tertiary consumers are the third level of consumers in the chain.
  • Some ecosystems have more levels, others fewer.
5) Energy Transfer and Arrow Direction
  • As we move up the food chain, energy is transferred from one level to the next.
  • However, most of the energy is lost between each level.
  • For example, about 1,000 J of energy in grass might give only about 100 J to mice and around 20 J to an owl.
  • In diagrams, the arrows between organisms show the direction of energy flow (from the food to the organism that eats it).
6) Interactions in Ecosystems
  • One of the most important ideas in ecology is that organisms interact and influence each other.
  • Changes in the population of one species can affect the populations of other species.
  • Predators and their prey often show linked patterns over time.
7) Predator–Prey Cycles
  • A predator–prey cycle shows how the populations of a predator and its prey change over time.
  • In the example, the prey is the field mouse and the predator is the owl.
  • Both populations cycle up and down rather than staying constant.
  • The predator population changes always lag behind the prey population slightly.
  • Because of this lag, the cycles are said to be out of phase with each other.
8) Phase 1 – Low Predator, Rising Prey
  • At first, the owl population is low.
  • With few predators, the mouse population can increase because fewer mice are being eaten.
  • This leads to a peak in the prey population later on.
9) Phase 2 – More Prey, Then More Predators
  • When there are now many mice, there is plenty of food for the owls.
  • The predator population (owls) then begins to increase.
  • Eventually, there are too many predators, and they eat a lot of the mice.
  • This causes the mouse population to decline.
10) Phase 3 – Falling Predator Numbers and Repeating Cycles
  • With fewer mice around, there is now less food for the owls.
  • The owl population then begins to decline as well.
  • With fewer predators again, the mouse population can rise once more.
  • This brings the system back to a similar starting point, so the cycle repeats again and again.
  • The key reason for the cycles is that populations take time to increase or decrease, often over several generations.
