Land Use: Deforestation & Peat Bogs

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Land as a limited resource
  • Land is limited, but humans depend on it for cities, quarrying, farming, and waste disposal.
  • When people convert land for human use, less habitat remains for other organisms.
  • Land conversion can trigger knock-on environmental effects that extend beyond the site itself.
2) Deforestation: what it is and why it happens
  • Deforestation means cutting down forests.
  • Main drivers include clearing land for:
    Cattle ranching Growing animal feed Crops (e.g. rice)
  • Forests may also be cleared to grow crops for biofuels (plant-based fuels).
  • Cutting trees to obtain wood is called logging (for building materials or fuel).
  • Deforestation is happening worldwide, but is especially significant in tropical rainforest regions.
3) Three major problems caused by deforestation
  • Less carbon dioxide removed: fewer trees means less photosynthesis, so less CO2 is taken from the atmosphere.
  • More carbon dioxide released: forests are sometimes cleared by burning, which releases stored carbon as CO2 and creates heavy smoke that harms animals and people.
  • Habitat loss: forests often have very high biodiversity. Removing them destroys habitats, reduces biodiversity, and can drive species extinction.
Deforestation → climate + air quality + biodiversity impacts • Fewer trees → less CO2 uptake • Burning/decay → more CO2 released • Habitat destruction → biodiversity falls, extinction risk rises
4) Peat bogs: how peat forms and why it matters
  • Peat bogs have soil that is acidic and waterlogged.
  • These conditions stop many decomposer microorganisms from surviving:
    • High acidity is hostile to microbes.
    • Waterlogging blocks air from entering the soil, so there is not enough oxygen for aerobic respiration.
  • As a result, dead plants only partially decay and build up into peat.
  • Peat stores the carbon the plants absorbed, so it helps reduce CO2 in the atmosphere and limits global warming.
5) Destroying peat bogs: drainage, extraction, and consequences
  • Peat bogs are often drained to create farmland for cattle or crops.
  • When water is removed, oxygen returns and decomposers can work again, so stored peat carbon is released as CO2.
  • Peat can also be extracted for:
    Burning as fuel Garden compost
  • Either way, the result is more CO2 released.
  • Peat bog destruction is happening faster than new bogs can form, and it also causes habitat loss, reducing biodiversity and increasing extinction risk.
Key takeaway • Land conversion can raise CO2 and reduce biodiversity. • Forest loss removes a CO2 sink and can release CO2 via burning. • Peat bogs are carbon stores; draining/extraction releases CO2 and destroys habitats.
