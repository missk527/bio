Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Gametes and sexual reproduction
  • Sexual reproduction requires gametes such as sperm and egg cells.
  • Gametes contain half the genetic material of a normal cell.
  • These cells are called haploid cells.
2) Haploid and diploid cells
  • When two gametes fuse, they form a diploid cell.
  • Diploid cells contain two sets of genetic information, one from each parent.
  • This new cell can develop into a whole organism.
3) Chromosomes in human cells
  • Chromosomes are structures that carry genetic information.
  • Humans have 23 types of chromosomes.
  • Each type has two copies: one maternal and one paternal, giving a total of 46 chromosomes.
4) DNA replication
  • Before meiosis, DNA is replicated.
  • Each chromosome forms an X-shape with two identical arms.
  • These arms are copies of the same genetic information.
5) First division (Meiosis I)
  • Chromosomes line up in pairs at the center of the cell.
  • The arrangement of maternal and paternal chromosomes is random.
  • The pairs are separated into two cells, creating different genetic combinations.
6) Second division (Meiosis II)
  • Chromosomes line up again in the center.
  • The two arms of each chromosome are pulled apart.
  • The cell divides again, producing four cells.
7) Genetic variation and gametes
  • The four cells produced are genetically unique.
  • Each cell is a haploid gamete with 23 chromosomes.
  • These gametes develop into sperm or egg cells.
8) Fertilization and development
  • A sperm and egg fuse during fertilization to form a diploid cell.
  • This cell divides by mitosis to form an embryo.
  • The embryo develops into a fetus and eventually a fully grown organism.
