Photosynthesis

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

What photosynthesis is and where it happens
  • Plants must carry out photosynthesis to grow and survive.
  • Photosynthesis mainly happens in the leaves, inside subcellular structures called chloroplasts.
  • Chloroplasts contain chlorophyll, a pigment that absorbs light energy.
Inputs and outputs
  • Light energy is used to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen.
  • Word equation: carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen (with light).
  • Symbol equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ (with light).
Energy transfer and reaction type
  • Because the reaction needs energy from the surroundings (light), photosynthesis is an endothermic reaction.
  • Energy is transferred from the environment to the chloroplasts by light.
How plants get the reactants
  • Carbon dioxide diffuses into leaves through the stomata.
  • Water is absorbed from the soil by the roots and transported to leaves in the xylem.
Why glucose is the key product
  • The main useful product for plants is glucose.
  • Oxygen is produced as a by-product; plants may use some for respiration, but there is plenty in the air.
Five main uses of glucose
  • Respiration: glucose is broken down to release energy (like a stored “battery” of light energy).
  • Cellulose: many glucose molecules join to make cellulose for cell wall strength.
  • Starch: glucose is converted to starch for long-term storage (compact and insoluble, so it does not draw in water by osmosis).
  • Amino acids & proteins: glucose combines with nitrate ions (from soil) to form amino acids, which then form proteins.
  • Oils and fats: glucose can be converted into oils/fats for energy storage, especially in seeds.
Starch storage and when it matters
  • Plants store starch so it can be broken back down into glucose when photosynthesis is low (e.g., at night or in winter).
Rate of photosynthesis
  • Plant growth rate often depends on how fast photosynthesis happens.
  • Main limiting factors: light intensity, temperature, carbon dioxide concentration, and chlorophyll amount.
