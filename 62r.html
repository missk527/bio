Biodiversity: Importance, Threats, Pollution & Solutions

Notes (rewritten in English from the speech)

What is biodiversity?
  • Biodiversity is the variety of different species on Earth or within a particular ecosystem.
  • A rainforest has much higher biodiversity than an average field because it contains a wider range of plants, animals, bacteria, and fungi.
Species variety Ecosystems Rainforest > field
Why biodiversity matters: ecosystem stability
  • High biodiversity helps make ecosystems stable.
  • If one species is lost (e.g., through disease and extinction), the ecosystem can still function because many other species and relationships remain.
Why biodiversity matters to humans
  • Many species provide services we rely on, such as pollination.
  • About 200,000 animal species act as pollinators, helping pollinate crops and plants (e.g., apples and avocados).
  • Over half of new medical drugs are derived from living organisms in some way, so extinctions could mean losing future medicines.
  • Human survival and wellbeing depend on maintaining good biodiversity worldwide.
How humans reduce biodiversity
  • Human activities often damage environments, reducing biodiversity and the benefits it provides.
  • Two major drivers are:
    • Population growth (more people)
    • Higher per-person impact (greater consumption and demand for resources)
Population growth and resource demand
  • Over the last ~1000 years, human population increased from about 300 million to about 7.7 billion (as described in the speech).
  • Higher standards of living and consumer goods (phones, clothes, cars) increase demand for raw materials and energy.
  • We often use natural resources faster than they can be replaced, risking depletion.
Habitat destruction and extinction
  • Extracting resources can destroy habitats by clearing ecosystems.
  • Deforestation is one example of habitat removal.
  • Overexploitation (e.g., poaching) can drive species to extinction (example given: western black rhino).
Pollution and biodiversity loss
  • Humans produce waste individually (rubbish, sewage) and collectively (industrial production).
  • Pollution can enter water, land, and air:
    • Sewage and toxic industrial chemicals can pollute rivers, lakes, and oceans.
    • Farming chemicals can harm aquatic ecosystems.
    • Landfill and stored wastes can leak toxins into ecosystems.
    • Burning fossil fuels and industry release gases like sulfur dioxide, which can harm organisms and cause acid rain.
  • Pollution can harm organisms directly or indirectly, changing ecosystems and reducing biodiversity.
Greenhouse gases and solutions
  • Greenhouse gases may not harm organisms directly but contribute to global warming, which affects biodiversity.
  • The speech notes these problems can be addressed with changes in attitudes and through new technologies and techniques.
