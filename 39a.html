Nervous System & Reflex Arc

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Why communication systems are needed

Large multicellular organisms must coordinate actions and respond to internal and external changes. The body uses two main communication systems: the nervous system (fast) and the endocrine system (slower, hormone-based).

2) What a neuron is adapted to do

Neurons (nerve cells) are specialized to carry electrical impulses. Their long, thin shape and branched ends help signals travel quickly and connect with many other cells.

3) Neuron structure in simple terms

Typical neurons have dendrites to receive signals, a cell body, a long axon to carry impulses, and nerve endings that communicate with the next cell.

4) Synapses and neurotransmitters

Neurons do not usually touch. At a synapse, an arriving electrical impulse triggers release of chemical messengers (neurotransmitters) that diffuse across the gap and start a new impulse in the next neuron.

5) The central nervous system (CNS)

The CNS is the brain and spinal cord. It processes sensory information, makes decisions, and sends instructions to the rest of the body.

6) Sensory neurons and receptors

Receptors detect stimuli (e.g., temperature, pressure, CO₂ levels). Sensory neurons carry impulses from receptors to the CNS.

7) Motor neurons and effectors

After processing, the CNS sends impulses along motor neurons to effectors—usually muscles (contract) or glands (secrete).

8) What the nervous system achieves

Together, receptors, neurons, the CNS, and effectors let the body detect change, interpret information, and produce coordinated responses.

9) Reflex arcs

A reflex arc is the pathway for rapid, automatic (unconscious) responses such as withdrawing from pain or blinking. Reflexes help prevent injury by reducing reaction time.

10) Reflex arc pathway example

Stimulus → receptor → sensory neuron → relay neuron in spinal cord → motor neuron → effector (muscle) → response. Synapses occur between each neuron, where the signal briefly becomes chemical.
