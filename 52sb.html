Notes (Key points from the speech)

What is selective breeding?
  • Selective breeding = choosing the best individuals (plants/animals) and breeding them to get even better offspring.
  • Humans have done this for thousands of years, since the start of agriculture (farming).
  • Used widely today for many traits.
Common aims / traits
  • Livestock: cows with high meat or milk yield.
  • Crops: disease-resistant varieties.
  • Pets: friendly dogs and cats.
  • Ornamentals: plants with large/unusual flowers.
How the process works
  • Start with your existing stock (e.g., all tomato plants in a field).
  • Select individuals with the desired characteristics (e.g., plants with many large tomatoes).
  • Breed those selected together to produce the next generation.
  • Repeat for many generations, always picking the best → gradual improvement.
Evidence of change over time
  • Compare wild sweet corn (~10,000 years ago) with modern sweet corn: dramatic changes in size and form.
  • Similar changes are seen across many plants due to repeated selective breeding for bigger, better, juicier crops.
Main drawback — reduced gene pool
  • Gene pool = the collection of all different alleles in a population.
  • Selective breeding favours certain alleles and removes others → the gene pool becomes smaller over time.
  • Inbreeding risk: the “best” individuals may be closely related, increasing inherited defects and disease susceptibility.
  • Example consequence: some dog breeds (e.g., German shepherds, Rottweilers) are prone to health conditions.
Another drawback — lower variation & vulnerability
  • A small gene pool means less variation within the population.
  • If a new pathogen infects one plant, it may infect almost all, risking the entire crop.
