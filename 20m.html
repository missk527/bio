Metallic Bonding & Alloys

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

What metallic bonding is
  • Metallic bonding is the bonding between metal atoms in a solid metal.
  • It helps explain typical metal properties like strength, high melting/boiling points, and good conductivity.
  • Compared with other bonding types: metals + non-metals often form ionic bonds, non-metals + non-metals form covalent bonds, and metals + metals form metallic bonds.
Structure of a solid metal
  • A solid metal is a giant lattice of atoms arranged in a regular pattern.
  • When metal atoms come together, they release their outer-shell electrons into a shared “sea” of electrons.
  • The metal atoms become positive ions because they have lost negative electrons.
Delocalised electrons and the metallic bond
  • The released electrons are delocalised (free to move throughout the structure).
  • There is strong electrostatic attraction between the positive ions and the negative delocalised electrons.
  • These attractions hold the lattice together: this is metallic bonding.
Why metals are strong and have high melting/boiling points
  • Because the attraction acts in all directions through the lattice, metallic bonding is generally strong.
  • It often takes a lot of energy to overcome these forces, so many metals have high melting points and high boiling points.
  • That is why most metals are solid at room temperature.
Why metals conduct electricity and heat
  • Delocalised electrons can move through the lattice and carry electric charge, so metals are good electrical conductors.
  • These electrons also transfer energy efficiently, so metals are good thermal conductors.
Malleability: strong but bendable
  • Metals are often malleable: they can be bent or hammered into shapes (like thin sheets).
  • In a pure metal, the regular layers of ions can slide over each other without breaking the metallic bond.
  • This sliding is why metals can change shape even though the bonding is strong.
Alloys: harder than pure metals
  • An alloy contains two or more elements (often two metals, or a metal plus a non-metal).
  • Alloys are designed so atoms are different sizes, which disrupts the regular layers in a pure metal.
  • With the layers disrupted, they cannot slide as easily, making the alloy harder and less likely to bend.
Example: steel
  • Steel is mostly iron with about 1–2% carbon.
  • It may also include elements such as chromium, manganese, or vanadium to adjust properties.
  • Steel’s improved strength makes it useful in structures and vehicles such as cars and planes.
