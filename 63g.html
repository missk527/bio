Notes (Key points from the speech)

Global warming vs. climate change
  • Global warming refers to the long-term rise in Earth’s average temperature.
  • Climate change refers to the broader impacts of that warming on long-term weather patterns (the climate).
The atmosphere and the greenhouse effect
  • Earth is surrounded by a protective layer of gases called the atmosphere.
  • The atmosphere acts like an insulating blanket, helping Earth stay at a stable, livable temperature.
  • Incoming sunlight passes through the atmosphere and warms the surface.
  • The warmed surface sends energy back upward as radiation, which is absorbed and re-emitted by gas molecules in many directions.
  • This repeated absorb–re-emit process keeps more heat near Earth than if there were no atmosphere.
Greenhouse gases
  • Gases that trap heat are called greenhouse gases.
  • Examples mentioned: carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and water vapor.
  • The natural greenhouse effect is essential for life, but strengthening it leads to warming.
What causes today’s extra warming?
  • Over Earth’s history, greenhouse strength has increased and decreased many times.
  • Right now, the main driver is human activity releasing large amounts of CO₂ and methane into the atmosphere.
  • As these gases accumulate, the greenhouse effect intensifies and the planet warms.
How much has Earth warmed?
  • In roughly the last 100 years, Earth has warmed by almost 1°C.
  • Without major action, temperatures are projected to rise further.
Climate change impacts: extreme weather
  • Climate describes long-term patterns of weather, not day-to-day conditions.
  • Warming can make rare events like droughts, hurricanes, and floods both more common and more severe.
Climate change impacts: sea level rise
  • Sea levels rise for two main reasons:
    • Melting ice adds more water to the oceans.
    • Thermal expansion: warmer ocean water expands, increasing volume.
  • Consequences include seasonal flooding and, in some places, the submersion of islands and coastal towns.
Climate change impacts: ecosystems and biodiversity
  • As temperature and rainfall patterns shift, species may no longer be suited to their habitats.
  • Some organisms may adapt or migrate to better conditions (often toward the poles where it is cooler).
  • Many species cannot adapt or move fast enough, so biodiversity may decline sharply in coming decades.
