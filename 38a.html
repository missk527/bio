Homeostasis

Video link: https://youtu.be/tFyVrHF__0o

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) What homeostasis means
  • Homeostasis is the regulation of conditions inside the body to keep a stable internal environment.
  • The body responds to changes in internal and external conditions (e.g., cold weather or hot deserts).
  • Levels are kept within small limits (they can fluctuate, but not too much).
2) Why cells need stable conditions
  • Cells work best within a narrow range of conditions (temperature, pH, water, glucose, etc.).
  • If conditions move too far from the optimum, enzymes and cell processes stop working properly.
3) Automatic control systems: the 3 parts
  • Receptors detect a change (stimulus), such as a rise or fall in temperature.
  • Coordination centres (brain, spinal cord, pancreas) process the information and decide on a response.
  • Effectors carry out the response (usually muscles or glands).
4) How the body sends signals
  • The nervous system uses fast, precise electrical impulses along nerves for quick responses.
  • The endocrine system uses hormones carried in the blood; it is slower, longer-lasting, and more general.
  • Hormones only affect target cells that have the correct receptors.
5) Negative feedback (the key mechanism)
  • Negative feedback reverses a change to bring a level back toward normal.
  • If a level is too high, the response makes it decrease.
  • If a level is too low, the response makes it increase.
6) Example: temperature control
  • If you enter a cold room, receptors detect the drop in temperature and the brain coordinates a response.
  • Effectors (muscles) cause shivering to generate heat and raise temperature.
  • If you get too hot, a different response happens: sweating helps cool you down.
  • This creates a repeating feedback loop that keeps body temperature near normal.
