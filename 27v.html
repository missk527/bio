27. Viruses & Viral Diseases

Key Notes (English)

Adapted from the provided speech. Focus: what viruses are, how they cause disease, and examples of measles, HIV and tobacco mosaic virus (TMV).

1) What Are Viruses?
  • Viruses are not cells and are generally considered non-living.
  • However, we usually still treat them as organisms in biology.
  • They are extremely small – around 10,000 times smaller than animal cells.
  • They cannot reproduce by themselves outside a host cell.
2) How Viruses Reproduce
  • Viruses must get inside the cells of another organism.
  • Once inside, they use the cell’s own machinery to make many copies of themselves.
  • When enough copies are made, the virus can burst the host cell.
  • The newly made viruses are then released and must find new cells to infect.
3) Why Viruses Make Us Ill
  • This process of infecting and then bursting our cells damages our tissues.
  • Cell damage is one of the main reasons that viral infections make us feel ill.
  • Different viruses target different cells or organisms, causing different diseases.
4) Measles – Symptoms and Spread
  • Disease Measles is caused by the measles virus.
  • It is spread by droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes.
  • Typical symptoms:
    • A red rash over much of the body.
    • A fever – high temperature and feeling very unwell.
  • Measles can be very serious and sometimes even fatal.
  • In the UK, measles is now rare because most people are vaccinated when they are young.
5) HIV – What It Is
  • Virus HIV stands for Human Immunodeficiency Virus.
  • This tells us that:
    • It infects humans.
    • It causes a deficient (weakened) immune system.
  • When we say “HIV”, we are referring to the virus itself, not the disease.
6) HIV – Transmission and Early Infection
  • HIV can be spread by:
    • Sexual contact, such as unprotected sex.
    • Exchange of bodily fluids, for example blood when people share needles.
  • When someone first catches HIV, they may develop flu-like symptoms for a few weeks:
    • Fever
    • Tiredness
    • Aches
  • After this, they often feel better and may think nothing is wrong.
7) HIV to AIDS and Treatment
  • Even when the person feels well, HIV continues to damage the immune system over time.
  • Eventually, the immune system becomes so weak that the person starts to get:
    • Unusual or opportunistic infections
    • Sometimes certain cancers
  • At this stage, they are said to have developed AIDS (Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome).
  • AIDS describes the disease/syndrome in which the immune system can no longer cope.
  • In the past, this often led to death from infections or cancer.
  • Now, we have antiretroviral drugs that stop HIV from replicating in the body.
  • If people have early and ongoing access to these medications, they can often live normal lives.
8) Tobacco Mosaic Virus (TMV)
  • Plant virus Tobacco mosaic virus (TMV) is a virus that infects plants.
  • It affects certain species such as:
    • Tobacco plants
    • Tomato plants
  • It does not infect all plant species.
9) Symptoms and Effects of TMV
  • TMV causes discoloured patches on the leaves.
  • These patches form a mosaic pattern, which is why it is called “mosaic”.
  • In the discoloured areas, photosynthesis cannot take place properly.
  • Because photosynthesis is reduced, the plant cannot make enough sugars.
  • As a result, the plant’s growth is poor and overall yield is lower.
10) Overall Summary
  • Viruses are tiny, non-living particles that can only reproduce inside host cells.
  • They damage the host by infecting and then bursting cells.
  • Examples of viral diseases include:
    • Measles – human viral disease with rash and fever, prevented by vaccination.
    • HIV – human virus that can lead to AIDS if untreated.
    • TMV – plant virus that damages leaves and reduces growth.
