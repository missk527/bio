Enzymes: Lock & Key vs Induced Fit

Key Notes (English)

Why cells need enzymes
  • Cells perform many chemical reactions every day, but most reactions are naturally slow.
  • Heating up cells is dangerous: it uses too much energy, can damage cells, and speeds up unwanted reactions.
  • A better solution is a catalyst — a substance that speeds up a reaction without being used up.
  • Enzymes are biological catalysts made of proteins.
Structure of an enzyme
  • Enzymes are long chains of amino acids folded into unique shapes.
  • Each enzyme has an active site with a specific shape for a specific substrate.
  • If a substrate does not fit the active site, the reaction will not be catalyzed.
Substrates and products
  • A substrate is the molecule an enzyme acts on.
  • Enzymes may break a substrate into smaller products or combine substrates into a larger product.
  • Enzymes speed up the reaction but are not used up, so they can work again and again.
Lock & Key Model
  • The original idea: the substrate fits perfectly into the active site like a key into a lock.
  • This explains enzyme specificity: only the correct substrate fits.
Induced Fit Model
  • A more realistic explanation.
  • The active site is not a perfect fit at first.
  • The enzyme changes shape slightly to fit the substrate more closely.
  • This helps the reaction happen efficiently.
