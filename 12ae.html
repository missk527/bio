Notes (Key points from the speech)

Enzymes recap
  • Enzymes are biological catalysts: they speed up chemical reactions without being used up.
  • They have a specific active site that binds a matching substrate.
Temperature affects enzyme rate
  • As temperature rises, particle kinetic energy increases → more frequent successful collisions → reaction rate rises.
  • Beyond about 37 °C (typical for many human enzymes), rate falls rapidly.
  • High temperatures break bonds that hold the enzyme’s 3D shape → the active site changes shape.
  • If the shape change is extreme, the substrate no longer fits and the enzyme is denatured (irreversible loss of function).
  • Optimum temperature: the temperature at which a given enzyme works best (rate peaks). Different enzymes have different optima.
pH affects enzyme rate
  • pH measures acidity/alkalinity.
  • If pH is too low or too high for that enzyme, bonds are disrupted and the active site shape shifts.
  • Small changes → substrate still fits, but less well → rate falls. Large changes → denaturation.
  • Optimum pH depends on location:
    • Most human enzymes: around pH 7 (neutral).
    • Stomach enzymes: around pH 2 (acidic), matching stomach conditions.
Calculating rate of reaction
  • General idea: rate = change in amount ÷ time.
  • Units must match the question (e.g., cm³ s⁻¹, g min⁻¹).
  • Example: an enzyme reaction makes 30 cm³ of gas in 2 minutes. Convert time to seconds: 2 min × 60 = 120 s → rate = 30 ÷ 120 = 0.25 cm³ s⁻¹.
