The Brain

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) What the brain does
  • The brain contains billions of interconnected nerve cells (neurons) and stores our thoughts and memories.
  • It controls complex behaviour, from everyday choices (like what to wear) to skills like running and reading.
2) The brain and the central nervous system
  • The central nervous system (CNS) is made of the brain and spinal cord.
  • Aside from simple reflexes, the brain makes most important decisions and coordinates responses.
3) Cerebral cortex (cerebrum) and hemispheres
  • The large wrinkled outer region is the cerebral cortex (or cerebrum).
  • It is linked to higher functions such as consciousness, intelligence, memory, and language, and also processes senses like vision and hearing.
  • The cortex is split into two hemispheres: the left hemisphere mainly controls muscles on the right side of the body, and the right hemisphere controls the left side.
4) Other key brain regions
  • The cerebellum (at the back) controls balance and muscle coordination, helping us stand and move smoothly.
  • The hypothalamus helps regulate internal conditions like body temperature and sends signals to the pituitary gland.
  • The brain stem connects the brain to the spinal cord; the medulla controls vital unconscious functions such as breathing and heart rate.
5) How scientists study the brain
  • Brain-damage (lesion) studies: linking lost abilities (e.g., after a stroke) to the damaged region helps infer that region’s function.
  • Electrical stimulation: stimulating a specific area can trigger effects like muscle movement or changes in vision, revealing what that area influences.
  • Brain scanning is widely used today to examine structure and activity.
6) Brain scans and why treatment is difficult
  • CT scans use X‑rays and are often used to detect damage.
  • PET scans use radioactive tracers, and MRI uses strong magnetic fields; both can help measure brain activity.
  • Treatment is challenging because many problems can occur (tumours, infection, trauma, mental illness), the brain is protected by the skull and is fragile, and the brain’s complexity is not fully understood—making both surgery and drug treatment difficult.
