Cycles in Nature: Water Cycle & Carbon Cycle

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Recycling atoms on Earth
  • Life on Earth has existed for billions of years because the planet recycles key substances.
  • Examples of recycled substances:
    Molecules like water (H₂O) Carbon dioxide (CO₂) Atoms like nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon
  • Because of continuous recycling, the same atoms can be used again and again in many different organisms over millions of years.
Amazing fact • You contain roughly 6 × 10²⁷ atoms (about six octillion). • Many of those atoms have previously been in: - oceans - volcanoes - other animals - other humans • They have been recycled and are now inside you.
  • Different substances have different cycles.
  • In this topic we focus on:
    The water cycle The carbon cycle
2) Water cycle – evaporation and transpiration
  • Imagine an environment with land and water (lakes, rivers, oceans).
  • Because it is a cycle, you can start anywhere, but we often begin with evaporation.
  • Energy from the Sun heats water and causes it to evaporate.
Evaporation in the water cycle • Liquid water → water vapour (gas) in the air. • Sources: - lakes, rivers, oceans - puddles, soil water
  • Plants also release water vapour from their leaves; this is called transpiration.
Transpiration • Water evaporates from plant leaves. • This adds more water vapour to the air.
3) Water cycle – condensation, precipitation & return to land
  • As water vapour builds up in the atmosphere, it begins to cool down and condense.
Condensation • Water vapour → tiny liquid droplets. • Droplets form clouds high in the sky. • Winds can blow clouds from one region to another.
  • Eventually the water returns to Earth as liquid water in the form of rain.
  • This falling water is called precipitation.
After precipitation • Rain can: - soak into the soil, - flow into rivers, - be taken up by plant roots. • The water can then be used again and eventually re-evaporates. • The cycle repeats continuously.
4) Carbon cycle – main carbon stores
  • The carbon cycle is more complex and involves many living organisms.
  • A good way to remember it is to think about the main stores of carbon and how carbon moves between them.
Major carbon stores 1) Atmosphere – as carbon dioxide (CO₂). 2) Plants – carbon locked in biological molecules (e.g. glucose, starch). 3) Soil – bacteria and microorganisms in warm, moist conditions. 4) Fossil fuels – underground (coal, oil, natural gas). 5) Animals – carbon in biological molecules (e.g. proteins, fats, carbohydrates).
5) Photosynthesis, feeding and respiration
  • The most important process in the carbon cycle is photosynthesis.
Photosynthesis • Green plants and algae take in CO₂ from the air. • They convert it into biological molecules such as glucose. • Carbon becomes locked inside plant tissues.
  • Once carbon is in plants it can:
    • return to the atmosphere as CO₂ through respiration in plants, or
    • be passed to animals that eat the plants.
  • Animals also carry out respiration and release CO₂ back into the atmosphere.
Respiration • Plants respire. • Animals respire. → Both processes release carbon dioxide into the atmosphere.
6) Decomposition, fossil fuels and combustion
  • When plants and animals die, their carbon-rich bodies enter the soil.
  • Two main things can then happen:
Path 1 – Decomposition in aerobic conditions • Microorganisms in warm, moist, oxygen-rich soil decay the dead organisms. • They break them into smaller pieces. • During microbial respiration, carbon is released as CO₂ back into the air.
Path 2 – Formation of fossil fuels • If dead organisms avoid normal decay and end up in anaerobic (low-oxygen) conditions: - they may be slowly converted into fossil fuels. • Examples: coal, oil, natural gas.
  • Humans extract these fossil fuels and burn them.
Combustion • Burning fossil fuels releases CO₂ back into the atmosphere. • Burning plant material (logs, biofuels) also releases CO₂.
  • Overall, the carbon cycle describes how carbon is continually stored and moved between the air, living things, the soil and fossil fuels.
