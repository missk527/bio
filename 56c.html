Biological Classification & Evolutionary Trees

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

Why classification matters
  • Earth hosts millions of species; classification gives us a shared system to name and group them.
  • Early naming relied on appearance (e.g., black vs. brown vs. polar bear), which led to inconsistent names across regions and did not show relationships.
  • Example: “grizzly bear” is a type of brown bear; “koala bear” and “panda bear” are not true bears in the same family context.
The Linnaean hierarchy (18th century)
  • Carl Linnaeus grouped organisms by shared characteristics and often skeletal structures.
  • Standard ranks (from broad to specific): Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species.
  • Names use mostly Latin so scientists worldwide can communicate.
  • Example (humans): Animalia → Chordata → Mammalia → Primates → Hominidae → Homo → sapiens.
Binomial nomenclature
  • Linnaeus proposed a two‑part scientific name: Genus + species (the binomial system).
  • Formatting rule: italics both words; capitalize the Genus; lowercase the species (e.g., Homo sapiens).
  • Why it helps: unique, concise, and avoids duplicate common names (many “Tom” vs. one Homo sapiens).
Three-domain system (1990s)
  • Carl Woese used rRNA sequence evidence to show deeper splits than kingdoms alone.
  • Added a level above kingdoms: the Domains — Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukaryota.
  • Eukaryota: all organisms with eukaryotic cells (animals, plants, fungi, protists).
  • Bacteria: single‑celled prokaryotes found nearly everywhere; often called “true bacteria.”
  • Archaea: prokaryotes distinct from bacteria; many live in extreme environments (hot springs, salt lakes) and are sometimes called “archaebacteria.”
Evolutionary trees (cladograms/phylogenies)
  • Show evolutionary relationships by linking species to their common ancestors.
  • A split (node) indicates one lineage diverging into two lineages.
  • Relative relatedness depends on how recent the shared ancestor is.
  • Example: Birds are closer to T. rex than to bats; bats and birds are still closer to each other than either is to spiders.
  • Tree layouts can vary in style but carry the same relationships.
  • Evidence sources: anatomy, DNA/RNA comparisons, and the fossil record.
