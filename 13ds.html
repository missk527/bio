13. Digestive System Overview

Key Notes (English)

Adapted from the provided speech. Focus: main organs of the human digestive system, what they do, and how food is broken down and absorbed.

1) Big Picture of Digestion
  • The digestive system breaks large, complex food into small, soluble molecules that can be absorbed into the blood and used by cells.
  • Main reasons we eat: to provide energy for movement and to keep metabolism and body processes running.
  • Digestion uses both physical (mechanical) breakdown and chemical breakdown by enzymes.
2) Key Nutrient Groups
  • Carbohydrates – e.g. starch and glucose; main source of energy.
  • Proteins – needed for growth, repair and enzymes.
  • Fats (lipids) – energy store, insulation and protection.
  • These nutrients are locked inside large molecules in big pieces of food → they must be digested first.
3) Mouth and Salivary Glands
  • Mechanical digestion Teeth chew food to break it into smaller pieces and increase surface area.
  • Saliva Released from salivary glands; makes food mixture more liquid and easier to swallow.
  • Saliva contains salivary amylase, an enzyme that begins the chemical breakdown of carbohydrates (starch).
4) Oesophagus (Gullet)
  • After swallowing, food travels down the gullet / oesophagus.
  • Muscle contractions push the food towards the stomach (this movement is called peristalsis, although the word is not stressed in the speech).
5) Stomach
  • The stomach is a muscular sac with three key roles:
  • 1. Muscular walls contract to churn food and mix it with digestive juices (mechanical + mixing).
  • 2. Produces pepsin, a protease enzyme that breaks down proteins into smaller chains.
  • 3. Produces hydrochloric acid (HCl) which:
    • kills bacteria in food, and
    • provides the acidic pH needed for pepsin to work properly.
  • After about an hour in the stomach, the partly digested food is released into the small intestine.
6) Small Intestine and Enzymes
  • The small intestine is the main site of digestion and absorption.
  • It produces some of its own digestive enzymes.
  • The pancreas makes most of the digestive enzymes and releases them into the small intestine as pancreatic juice.
  • Enzymes here continue the breakdown of carbohydrates, proteins and fats into very small, soluble molecules.
7) Liver, Gallbladder and Bile
  • Bile is made in the liver, but stored in the gallbladder until needed.
  • When food enters the small intestine, bile is released into it from the gallbladder.
  • Bile has two main roles:
    • Neutralisation Bile is alkaline, so it neutralises the acidic chyme from the stomach, creating a better pH for enzymes in the small intestine.
    • Emulsification of fats Bile breaks large fat droplets into many tiny droplets, increasing their surface area for lipase enzymes.
8) Absorption in the Small Intestine – Villi
  • Once food is fully digested into tiny molecules, they are absorbed into the blood through the lining of the small intestine.
  • The inner surface of the small intestine is covered with millions of villi (singular: villus), which look like small fingers.
  • Villi adaptations for efficient absorption:
    • Large surface area Many villi greatly increase the surface area, so absorption happens more quickly.
    • One-cell-thick walls The surface cells are only one cell thick → very short diffusion distance.
    • Good blood supply Dense capillary network maintains a concentration gradient and quickly carries absorbed nutrients away.
9) Large Intestine and Rectum
  • Even after absorption in the small intestine, there is still a lot of undigested material and water left.
  • This material passes into the large intestine.
  • The large intestine absorbs most of the remaining water, turning the watery mixture into more solid faeces.
  • Faeces are stored in the rectum until they are removed from the body.
10) Whole Process in Order
  • Food is taken into the mouth → chewed and mixed with saliva + salivary amylase.
  • Swallowed and moved down the oesophagus / gullet.
  • Enters the stomach → churned, mixed with pepsin and hydrochloric acid.
  • Passes into the small intestine → mixes with pancreatic juice from the pancreas and bile from the gallbladder (made in the liver).
  • Food is fully digested and then absorbed through the villi into the bloodstream.
  • Anything that cannot be digested plus lots of water moves into the large intestine → water is reabsorbed.
  • Remaining faeces are stored in the rectum before being removed from the body.
