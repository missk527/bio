Notes (Key points from the speech)

What is health?
  • Health is a state of physical and mental well‑being.
  • We don’t sit in two boxes (healthy / unhealthy) — we exist on a spectrum of health.
  • People differ because many factors affect health.
Main factors that support good health
  • Balanced diet
  • Regular exercise and adequate sleep
  • Access to medical care, especially preventive medicine such as vaccines
  • Lower levels of stress
What is disease?
  • Disease is a broad term for conditions that cause ill health.
  • Some diseases are linked to lifestyle factors (e.g., an unhealthy diet → type 2 diabetes).
  • Other diseases are not related to lifestyle (e.g., chickenpox, asthma).
Two big groups of diseases
  • Communicable diseases (a.k.a. infectious): can spread between people or between animals and people; caused by pathogens such as viruses, bacteria, parasites, fungi. Examples: common cold, malaria, meningitis.
  • Non‑communicable diseases: cannot spread between people; often develop slowly and last a long time. Examples: asthma, coronary heart disease, diabetes, cancer.
How different diseases interact
  • Weakened immunity → more infections: If a person’s immune system is impaired, they catch communicable diseases (e.g., colds, flu) more often.
  • Viruses linked to cancers: HPV (human papillomavirus) can cause cervical cancer. In the UK, most girls receive an HPV vaccine, greatly reducing risk.
  • Overactive immunity: Conditions such as skin rashes and asthma can result from the body’s immune system overreacting to a pathogen and damaging its own tissues.
  • Physical ↔ mental health: Serious physical illness can limit daily activities (going out, work, social life) and increase the risk of depression or chronic anxiety.
