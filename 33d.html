Drug Development & Testing: From Discovery to Clinical Trials

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Drug discovery: using nature as a starting point
  • Microorganisms and plants have evolved over millions of years to produce chemicals that can do useful jobs, including killing pathogens.
  • Scientists can take these natural substances and use them as medicines directly, or modify them in a lab first.
Natural products Pathogen-killing chemicals Lab modification
2) Three classic examples
  • Aspirin: a common painkiller that can also reduce fever; originally developed from a chemical in willow bark.
  • Digitalis: used to treat heart problems such as heart failure; developed from chemicals in foxglove plants.
  • Penicillin: discovered when Alexander Fleming noticed a fungus killing bacteria around it in a petri dish; named after the fungus genus Penicillium.
Big message • Nature can provide powerful drug candidates. • Penicillin became the first widely used antibiotic.
3) What drug testing must establish
  • Efficacy — how well the drug works (the size of the benefit).
  • Toxicity — how harmful it is (cell damage, side effects, risks).
  • Dosage — how much should be given to get benefits without unacceptable harm.
  • Often, a higher dose increases effect, but also increases side effects—so dosage decisions are a trade‑off.
4) Preclinical testing: cells/tissues then animals
  • Stage 1: test on human cells and tissues grown in a lab. This is quick and cheap for screening many substances.
  • Limitation: cell tests don’t fully show what happens in a whole organism or a specific organ system.
  • Stage 2: test on live animals to estimate whole‑body efficacy and toxicity (in the UK, drugs are tested on two mammal species).
  • Stages 1–2 are called preclinical because no humans are involved.
5) Clinical trials: humans, placebos, blinding, and peer review
  • Healthy volunteers first: begin with a very low dose, then increase slowly while doctors monitor side effects.
  • Goal: find the maximum safe dose before side effects become a problem.
  • Patients next: identify the optimum dose where benefits are maximized and harm is minimized.
  • Trials often use a placebo and are blind or double‑blind to reduce unconscious bias.
  • Finally, results are written up and peer reviewed by other scientists to check fairness and validity.
Placebo + blinding • Placebo looks like the drug but has no active ingredient. • Double-blind: neither doctors nor volunteers know who got what until after analysis.
