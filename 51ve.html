Notes (Key points from the speech)

Variation & phenotype
  • Individuals in a population differ — this is called variation.
  • A phenotype is the set of observable characteristics of an individual.
  • Phenotype results from the interaction of genes and environment.
Genes, proteins, genome
  • Genes code for proteins that build and run the body.
  • Your genome (all your genes) codes for the whole organism.
  • Except for identical twins, every person has a unique genome, so protein combinations — and appearances — differ.
Environmental effects
  • Environment modifies phenotype: diet, sleep, sunlight, training, etc.
  • Identical twins can differ in height or skin tone if their environments differ.
  • Most traits involve many genes and many environmental factors.
Mutations & genetic variation
  • A mutation is a change in the DNA code that may alter a protein.
  • Most mutations are neutral (no phenotype change); some are harmful; a few are beneficial.
  • Beneficial mutations may, for example, increase disease resistance or performance.
Natural selection & fitness
  • If a mutation improves survival or reproduction, carriers are more likely to leave offspring.
  • Darwin called this “survival of the fittest” and the process natural selection.
  • Fitness = how well a phenotype fits the environment (higher survival/reproductive success).
Evolution & speciation
  • Across generations, advantageous traits increase in frequency → evolution.
  • Given enough time, populations may diverge so much that they cannot interbreed to produce fertile offspring → a new species forms (speciation).
  • All modern species ultimately descend from simple life forms >3 billion years ago.
Evidence
  • Antibiotic resistance in bacteria shows evolution occurring in real time.
  • Fossil records also support gradual change over long timescales.
