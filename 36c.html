Notes (Key points from the speech)

What is respiration?
  • Most energy for life is released from glucose (made by plants in photosynthesis).
  • Respiration is an exothermic reaction that transfers energy from glucose.
  • Energy is not created — it is released/ transferred from the chemical store in glucose.
  • Respiration happens continuously in living cells.
Why organisms need the energy
  • Building larger molecules from smaller ones (e.g., amino acids → proteins).
  • Muscle contraction so organisms can move.
  • Maintaining body temperature (staying warm in cold environments).
Metabolism
  • Metabolism = all the chemical reactions in an organism that keep it alive.
  • Many reactions need energy and are often catalysed by enzymes.
Aerobic respiration (with oxygen)
  • Occurs when there is enough oxygen.
  • It is the most efficient way to transfer energy from glucose.
  • Happens in plants and animals, mainly in the mitochondria.
  • Word equation: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water.
  • Symbol equation: C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O.
  • This is the reverse of photosynthesis (photosynthesis makes glucose).
Anaerobic respiration in animals (without oxygen)
  • Used only when needed (e.g., sprinting when oxygen supply can’t meet demand).
  • Word equation: glucose → lactic acid.
  • Less efficient because glucose is not completely broken down without oxygen.
  • Lactic acid builds up and must be removed later.
Anaerobic respiration in plants & yeast (fermentation)
  • Glucose is converted to ethanol and carbon dioxide.
  • In yeast, this is called fermentation and is used in industry.
  • Bread making: CO2 makes air pockets so bread becomes light and fluffy.
  • Brewing: ethanol produced is used to make beer and wine.
Take‑home message
  • Respiration is essential because it provides energy for many cellular processes.
  • Aerobic respiration is preferred (efficient) but anaerobic is a useful backup when oxygen is limited.
