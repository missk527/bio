Earth's Atmosphere and Its Evolution

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) The atmosphere today and the idea of evolution
  • Earth’s atmosphere today is made of about 80% nitrogen and 20% oxygen, with carbon dioxide, water vapor, and the noble gases making up less than 1%.
  • Scientists explain this change using a well-supported theory about how the atmosphere has evolved over the last 4.6 billion years.
  • It is still called a theory because the events happened so long ago that the evidence, although strong, is limited.
2) The early atmosphere was rich in carbon dioxide
  • During the first billion years of Earth’s history, the planet was very hot and dry with intense volcanic activity.
  • Volcanoes released large amounts of carbon dioxide, water vapor, and nitrogen, plus smaller amounts of methane and ammonia.
  • This meant the early atmosphere was mainly carbon dioxide, similar to the atmospheres of Mars and Venus today.
Early atmosphere • Mainly carbon dioxide • Also water vapor and nitrogen • Small amounts of methane and ammonia • Caused largely by volcanic activity
3) Oceans formed and removed carbon dioxide
  • As Earth cooled, water vapor condensed into liquid water and formed the oceans.
  • Once oceans existed, large amounts of carbon dioxide dissolved in seawater.
  • This dissolved carbon dioxide later formed carbonates, which became sediments on the seabed.
4) Algae and plants changed the atmosphere
  • A major change began about 2.7 billion years ago when algae appeared.
  • Later, green plants evolved and made the process faster.
  • Both algae and plants carry out photosynthesis, which removes carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and releases oxygen.
  • Over billions of years, this caused atmospheric carbon dioxide levels to fall and oxygen levels to rise.
Photosynthesis (word idea) carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen Effect on atmosphere: • CO2 decreases • O2 increases
5) Carbon became locked away in rocks and fossil fuels
  • When algae, plants, and other organisms died, many sank to the bottom of the oceans and became buried by sediment.
  • Over millions of years, pressure and burial trapped the carbon in sedimentary rocks, oil, and natural gas.
  • Coal formed mainly from dead plant material.
  • Oil and natural gas formed mainly from dead plankton.
  • Limestone formed mainly from the calcium carbonate shells and skeletons of marine organisms.
Carbon stores in the ground • Coal → dead plants • Oil and natural gas → dead plankton • Limestone → shells and skeletons rich in calcium carbonate
