Genetic Engineering — Exam Notes & Quiz GM organisms · Insulin · Gene therapy · GM crops

Video + exam-focused notes + 40-question practice

Notes (Key points from the speech)

Core idea
  • Genetic engineering: identify a useful gene from one organism and transfer it into another so the recipient gains the trait.
  • This modifies the genome by adding a helpful gene → the organism is called genetically modified (GM).
  • Genes can be moved between different species, not just within the same species.
Examples you should know
  • Sheep engineered to produce therapeutic substances (drugs) in their milk → extract for treating disease.
  • Bacteria engineered to produce human insulin → harvested to treat diabetes.
  • GM crops: bigger/better fruit quality; resistance to diseases, insects, herbicides.
Gene therapy (fixing faulty genes)
  • Goal: give a person a healthy version of a faulty gene to correct an inherited disorder.
  • Challenge: the faulty gene is in all cells, so the new gene must be delivered to every cell — very difficult.
  • Potential solution: deliver at an early developmental stage (egg/embryo) so the healthy gene is copied to all cells as the person develops.
GM crops — pros & cons
  • Pros: desirable traits (yield, quality, resistance) → more food for less cost; can add missing nutrients (e.g., Golden rice with beta‑carotene to help prevent blindness).
  • Cons/uncertainties: long‑term health effects are not known with 100% certainty (no current evidence of harm, but uncertainty remains).
  • Ecology concern: GM plants could enter the wild and out‑compete local plants; considered unlikely because GM traits are tailored for farm fields, not survival in the wild.
How genes are transferred (method)
  • 1) Identify the desired gene; cut/isolate it with enzymes.
  • 2) Insert the gene into a vector: a virus or a bacterial plasmid (small circular DNA).
  • 3) Introduce the vector to the target organism (e.g., pig or plant); cells take up the vector and the gene.
  • 4) Cells begin making the protein encoded by the new gene → desired trait is expressed.
