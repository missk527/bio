Exercise Effects on the Body — Notes & Quiz Breathing · Heart · Oxygen Debt

Video + exam‑focused notes + 40‑question practice

Notes (Key points from the speech)

Exercise increases energy demand
  • During exercise, muscles contract more often and more strongly, so they need more energy.
  • Extra energy is supplied by an increased rate of cellular respiration.
Breathing rate and breath volume increase
  • Because respiration needs oxygen, the body must deliver more oxygen to muscles.
  • Breathing rate increases and the volume of each breath increases to bring in more oxygen.
  • This also uses energy because the muscles around the lungs work harder.
Heart rate increases
  • The heart pumps faster to move oxygen to the muscles more quickly.
  • Heart rate rises, which also requires energy.
  • The goal is to supply enough oxygen to keep using aerobic respiration.
When oxygen is limited
  • In very intense activity (e.g., sprinting or fast cycling), oxygen delivery may not meet demand.
  • Muscle cells then use anaerobic respiration as well.
  • Anaerobic respiration is less efficient than aerobic respiration, but it provides energy when oxygen is scarce.
Lactic acid and the burning sensation
  • Anaerobic respiration produces lactic acid.
  • Lactic acid can build up in tissues and cause a burning feeling in muscles after exercise.
Oxygen debt and recovery
  • After exercise, lactic acid must be removed by reacting it with oxygen.
  • The more lactic acid produced, the more oxygen is needed — this is called an oxygen debt.
  • Panting after a race helps repay the oxygen debt.
  • Blood transports lactic acid to the liver, where it reacts with oxygen and is converted back to glucose.
Investigating exercise effects
  • Main measurements: breathing rate and heart rate.
  • Breathing rate: count chest rises and falls for 1 minute.
  • Heart rate: measure pulse by placing two fingers on an artery (wrist or neck).
  • Exam questions often compare measurements before and after exercise and ask you to describe/explain changes.
Take-home message
  • Exercise increases respiration because muscles need more energy.
  • The body responds by increasing ventilation and heart rate, and may use anaerobic respiration when oxygen is limited.
