DNA, Chromosomes, Genes and Genome

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) DNA: the basic genetic material
  • DNA stands for deoxyribonucleic acid and it is the substance that carries genetic information.
  • It is the chemical from which all human genetic material is made.
  • DNA stores the instructions cells use to build and run the body.
2) DNA structure
  • DNA is a polymer, meaning it is made from many repeating subunits joined together.
  • It has two strands twisted around each other in a double helix.
  • The double helix can be described as a double-stranded spiral.
3) DNA packaging in cells
  • If the DNA from one cell were stretched out, it would be more than 2 metres long.
  • To prevent tangling and to fit inside the nucleus, DNA is tightly packed.
  • This packing helps organize genetic material efficiently inside each cell.
4) Chromosomes
  • Human DNA is divided into 46 tightly coiled sections called chromosomes.
  • Each chromosome is a compact package of DNA.
  • Chromosomes help keep long DNA molecules organized.
5) Chromosome pairs
  • There are 46 chromosomes in most body cells, but only 23 different types.
  • This is because chromosomes come in pairs, with one inherited from each parent.
  • For example, you have two chromosome 1s, two chromosome 2s, and so on.
6) Sex chromosomes
  • The 23rd pair is the sex chromosome pair.
  • Females usually have XX chromosomes, while males usually have XY chromosomes.
  • These chromosomes play a major role in biological sex determination.
7) Chromosome shape
  • Chromosomes are often drawn in an X shape, but this is only typical just before cell division.
  • They appear like this before processes such as mitosis or meiosis.
  • When cells are not dividing, chromosomes are usually less condensed.
8) Genes
  • A gene is a small section of DNA that contains instructions for making a specific protein.
  • You can think of a gene as a short segment located on a chromosome.
  • Different genes provide instructions for different proteins.
9) Genes and proteins
  • A gene acts as a code for a particular sequence of amino acids.
  • When amino acids are linked in the correct order, they form a protein.
  • Because proteins do so many jobs in the body, genes strongly influence cell function.
10) Protein variety and cell function
  • There are only 20 common amino acids, but they can combine in countless ways to make many proteins.
  • The proteins a cell makes help determine what kind of cell it becomes and what it does.
  • For example, red blood cells need lots of haemoglobin, while muscle cells need proteins for contraction.
11) Genome
  • The genome is the complete set of genetic material in an organism.
  • A person's genome includes all of their DNA, genes, and chromosomes together.
  • Each person's genome is unique, although close relatives usually share many similarities.
12) Why genomes matter
  • Scientists have worked out the complete human genome, meaning they know the full DNA code sequence.
  • This helps researchers identify genes linked to diseases and improve medical treatments.
  • Genome studies can also be used to trace human ancestry and migration patterns over time.
