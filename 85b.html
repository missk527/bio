85B. Pyramids of Biomass

Key Notes (Synced with Video)

0:00 - 0:52 | What Pyramids of Biomass Show
  • A pyramid of biomass shows the biomass at each trophic level in a food chain.
  • Biomass means the mass of living organisms.
  • In the example food chain, grass is the producer, rabbits are primary consumers, snakes are secondary consumers, and hawks are tertiary consumers.
  • Each bar shows the total mass of all organisms at that trophic level.
0:52 - 1:58 | Why Biomass Decreases Up the Pyramid
  • The bars usually get smaller as you move up the trophic levels.
  • This means the total biomass decreases at higher levels in the food chain.
  • Even if one snake is heavier than one rabbit, there are usually far fewer snakes, so total snake biomass is lower.
  • Only about 10% of biomass and energy is transferred to the next trophic level.
1:58 - 3:08 | Why Only Some Biomass Is Transferred
  • Organisms do not usually eat every part of the organism they consume.
  • Some eaten parts cannot be broken down or absorbed, so they are egested as feces.
  • Most absorbed nutrients are used to release energy through respiration rather than being stored as growth.
  • Biomass can be lost as waste products such as carbon dioxide and urea.
3:15 - 4:42 | Calculating Biomass Transfer Efficiency
  • Biomass transfer efficiency is the percentage of biomass passed to the next trophic level.
  • Efficiency = biomass transferred to the next level ÷ biomass available at the previous level × 100.
  • For rabbits to snakes: 15 kg ÷ 144 kg × 100 = 10.4%.
  • For snakes to hawks: 1.6 kg ÷ 15 kg × 100 = 10.7%.
