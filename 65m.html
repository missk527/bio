Biodiversity Conservation: Breeding Programs, Protected Areas & Sustainable Practices

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

Human impacts and why biodiversity falls
  • Human activities such as climate change, deforestation, and waste pollution damage ecosystems.
  • The overall result is a drop in biodiversity and a less stable, less resilient ecosystem.
Two main ways to reverse the trend
  • Reduce harmful practices (prevent the damage happening in the first place).
  • Reduce the impact of unavoidable practices and help ecosystems withstand pressure.
Breeding programs for endangered species
  • Captive breeding increases the numbers of endangered species to lower extinction risk.
  • Animals may later be reintroduced to the wild to join an existing population or start a new one.
  • Limitation: if the original habitat is still unsafe, the population can decline again after release.
Protected areas and habitat regeneration
  • Creating protected areas helps keep habitats safe so species can survive without constant human intervention.
  • Regenerating rare habitats (e.g., mangroves and coral reefs) supports many species and improves ecosystem health.
  • Well-managed protection can reduce the need for breeding programs because organisms remain safe in their natural environment.
Making necessary practices less harmful (example: farming)
  • Some practices are harmful but necessary (like farming for food), so conservation focuses on lowering their impact.
  • Governments can support this by laws and financial incentives (paying people to adopt wildlife-friendly methods).
  • Field margins (strips where wild plants can grow) and hedgerows (shrubs/trees along field edges) provide habitats and food sources.
  • These features increase the range of species that can live around farmland, boosting biodiversity.
Government regulation
  • Governments may set quotas on activities such as deforestation.
  • They can also limit how much carbon dioxide businesses are allowed to release.
What individuals can do
  • Recycle and reduce unnecessary consumption to cut the amount of waste sent to landfill.
  • Less landfill helps by:
    • keeping more land undamaged, and
    • reducing the risk of toxic chemicals leaking into the environment.
Why solutions are hard in practice
  • Cost: protecting biodiversity is expensive, and benefits are often underestimated by individuals, companies, and governments.
  • Lifestyle trade-offs: maintaining a high standard of living can require environmentally damaging activities.
  • Examples include using fertilisers and pesticides to grow enough food, and building large mines for rare metals used in electronics.
Challenges in developing countries
  • People may depend directly on the environment for survival.
  • Forest cutting may be for building homes, collecting firewood for cooking, or clearing land to grow crops.
