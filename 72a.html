Thermoregulation — Homeostasis and Temperature Control

Notes (rewritten in English from the given speech)

What thermoregulation is
  • Thermoregulation is the control of internal body temperature.
  • It is part of homeostasis, which means keeping the internal environment stable so cells can work properly.
  • The human body aims to keep temperature close to 37°C.
Homeostasis37°CStable internal conditions
Why body temperature must stay stable
  • 37°C is close to the optimum temperature for many enzymes in the body.
  • If body temperature falls too low, enzyme activity slows down.
  • If temperature rises too high, enzymes may denature and stop working properly.
What detects temperature changes
  • The thermoregulatory centre is found in the hypothalamus in the brain.
  • It acts like a thermostat for the body.
  • Temperature receptors in the skin and blood vessels detect changes and send information to the brain.
  • The brain decides whether the body is too hot or too cold overall.
How the body warms up
  • The body warms up by conserving heat and generating more heat.
  • Vasoconstriction: blood vessels near the skin surface get narrower, so less warm blood flows there and less heat is lost.
  • Erector muscles contract: hairs stand up and trap a thin layer of insulating air.
  • Shivering: muscles contract automatically, increasing respiration and releasing more heat energy.
How the body cools down
  • The body cools down by increasing heat loss.
  • Vasodilation: blood vessels near the skin surface widen, so more warm blood flows close to the skin and more heat is transferred to the surroundings.
  • Erector muscles relax: hairs lie flat, so less insulating air is trapped.
  • Sweating: sweat is released onto the skin. As it evaporates, it takes heat away from the body.

Evaporation needs energy, so sweat removes heat from the body and helps lower body temperature.
