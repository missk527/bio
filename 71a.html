Accommodation

Video + exam‑focused notes + interactive practice

Notes (Key points from the speech)

What accommodation is
  • Accommodation is a reflex that changes the refractive power of the eye’s lens so you can focus on near and distant objects.
  • Light must be focused onto the fovea (a spot on the retina) to form a sharp image.
  • The cornea does most of the refraction and bends light by a fixed amount; the lens fine‑tunes the focus.
Key structures that control lens shape
  • Ciliary muscle: a ring‑shaped muscle around the lens that can contract or relax.
  • Suspensory ligaments: fibres connecting the lens to the ciliary muscle; they cannot contract (not muscles) — they only become taut or slack.
  • Changing ligament tension changes lens curvature, which changes refractive power.
Focusing on near objects
  • Near objects send rays that arrive at the eye at a wider angle, so they need more refraction.
  • The ciliary muscle contracts inward (towards the lens).
  • Suspensory ligaments slacken → the lens becomes thicker / more curved.
  • A more curved lens is more powerful, so it refracts light more strongly onto the fovea.
Focusing on distant objects
  • Distant objects produce rays that are nearly parallel, so they need less refraction.
  • The ciliary muscle relaxes (moves away from the lens).
  • Suspensory ligaments become taut → the lens is stretched thinner / less curved.
  • A less curved lens has lower refractive power and still focuses light onto the retina because the cornea has done most of the bending.
Vision problems and how glasses help
  • Long‑sightedness (hyperopia): the eye cannot refract enough for near objects, so the image would form behind the retina → near objects look blurry.
  • Corrected using convex (converging) lenses to add extra refractive power.
  • Short‑sightedness (myopia): the eye refracts too much, so distant images form in front of the retina → distant objects look blurry.
  • Corrected using concave (diverging) lenses to reduce overall refraction so light focuses on the retina.
