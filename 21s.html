Notes (Key points from the speech)

Three states of matter
  • Matter commonly exists as solid, liquid, or gas.
  • Substances can change state when energy is added or removed (heating/cooling).
Particle theory model
  • To explain states, we use particle theory: treat each particle like a tiny solid, inelastic sphere.
  • The model focuses on spacing, movement, and forces of attraction between particles.
Solids
  • Particles have strong attractions and are packed closely in a fixed, regular lattice.
  • Solids keep a definite shape and definite volume, so they do not flow.
  • Particles are not still: they vibrate in place (jostling around their fixed positions).
Melting (solid → liquid)
  • Heating gives particles more energy, increasing vibration and weakening attractions.
  • At the melting point, particles have enough energy to break free from fixed positions.
  • The lattice collapses and the solid becomes a liquid.
Liquids
  • Attractions are weaker than in solids, so particles can move past each other.
  • Particles are arranged randomly but remain fairly close together (still compact).
  • Liquids have a definite volume but no fixed shape; they flow to fit a container.
Boiling / evaporation (liquid → gas)
  • Heating a liquid makes particles move faster, weakening attractions further.
  • At the boiling point, particles have enough energy to break bonds completely.
  • The liquid boils/evaporates into a gas.
Gases
  • Attractions are very weak, so particles move independently and are far apart.
  • Gases have no definite shape and no definite volume; they fill any container.
  • Gas particles travel in straight lines; “random motion” means their direction changes unpredictably after collisions with walls or other particles.
Heating and cooling gases
  • Heating a gas increases particle speed: it will expand in a flexible container (e.g., a balloon).
  • In a rigid container, heating increases pressure instead.
  • Cooling a gas reduces particle energy; attractions can pull particles together and the gas condenses to a liquid.
Freezing (liquid → solid)
  • Further cooling reduces energy so particles cannot overcome attractions.
  • More bonds form, fixing particles into place and the liquid freezes into a solid.
Limits of the model
  • Real particles are atoms, ions, or molecules — not solid, inelastic spheres.
  • The model does not describe the details of forces (how strong they are or how many act).
  • Even with limits, the model is a useful simplification for understanding states and changes of state.
