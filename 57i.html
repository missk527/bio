Notes (Key points rewritten from the speech)

Everything is connected
  • Organisms have relationships with other organisms (same species, predators, prey, neighbors) and with the environment itself.
  • Example (mice): breathe in O₂, breathe out CO₂; burrow soil → improves aeration/irrigation → better soil quality.
  • Changes ripple: if one part changes (e.g., mouse numbers fall), it can affect food for predators and soil quality.
Key terms you must know
  • Habitat: the place an organism lives (e.g., field, forest, basement).
  • Population: all individuals of one species in a habitat (e.g., all mice in the field).
  • Community: all populations of different species living together (e.g., mice, owls, plants in the field).
  • Biotic factors: living factors (e.g., food availability, number of predators).
  • Abiotic factors: non‑living factors (e.g., temperature, soil pH).
  • Ecosystem: interactions of the community with the non‑living environment (habitat + community + biotic/abiotic factors).
Competition
  • Resources are limited, so organisms compete.
  • Animals compete for territory (space), food, water, and mates.
  • Plants compete for light, space, water, and mineral ions from soil.
  • Competition occurs both between species (e.g., lions vs. hyenas) and within species (e.g., male deer for females).
Interdependence & food webs
  • Interdependence: all species depend on others in some way.
  • Food web: shows feeding relationships in a community.
  • Knock‑on effects: change to one species affects others (e.g., more mice → less grass for rabbits/grasshoppers; hawks may rise but also lose rabbits).
  • Goal: understand relationships and be able to predict effects of changes.
