Cell Cycle, Chromosomes & Mitosis

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

Why organisms need new cells
  • All multicellular eukaryotes (humans, plants, walruses, etc.) continually make new cells for growth, development, and repair.
  • Young organisms grow in size; older organisms replace cells that are lost (e.g., shedding skin cells).
The cell cycle at a glance
  • The cell cycle is a cell’s life from its formation to its division into two new cells.
  • Main stages to know:
    1. Growth – cell increases in size and number of subcellular structures (e.g., mitochondria, ribosomes).
    2. DNA replication – the genome is duplicated so each new cell will receive a full copy.
    3. Mitosis followed by cytokinesis – chromosomes separate, then the cytoplasm and membrane divide to form two daughter cells.
DNA packaging: from strands to chromosomes
  • When a cell is not dividing, DNA exists as long, extended strands.
  • As division approaches, DNA condenses into compact chromosomes (coiled packages of DNA).
  • Each chromosome carries many genes that control characteristics (e.g., eye color).
Chromosome sets and species differences
  • Eukaryotic animal cells have pairs of chromosomes—one copy from the mother and one from the father.
  • Humans have 23 pairs (46 total). Different species have different numbers, which helps explain why most different species cannot produce fertile offspring together.
Chromosome duplication before mitosis
  • Before division, each of the 46 human chromosomes is duplicated.
  • The duplicate stays attached to the original, forming an X-shaped structure of two identical sister chromatids (the two “arms”).
Mitosis: line up, separate, move apart
  • All chromosomes align at the cell’s center (the equator).
  • Spindle (cell) fibers attach from opposite sides and pull on each chromatid.
  • Each X is pulled apart so the sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles of the cell.
Cytokinesis and the outcome
  • Cytokinesis splits the cytoplasm and cell membrane.
  • Two genetically identical daughter cells form—each has the same DNA as the parent cell and as each other.
  • These cells then support growth, development, or repair and can re-enter the cycle.
