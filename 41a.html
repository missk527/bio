Blood Glucose Regulation — Insulin, Glucagon & Negative Feedback

Notes (rewritten in English from the given speech)

What blood glucose concentration means
  • Blood glucose concentration means the amount of glucose (sugar) in the bloodstream.
  • The body must keep blood glucose at a balanced level so cells always have enough glucose for respiration.
  • If blood glucose becomes too high, it can damage body tissues.
  • If it becomes too low, cells may not get enough glucose for energy release.

What happens after eating carbohydrates
  • After a meal rich in carbohydrates, digestion breaks them down into many glucose molecules.
  • These glucose molecules are absorbed into the bloodstream.
  • As a result, blood glucose concentration rises above the normal level.
  • This rise is detected by the pancreas, which lies behind the stomach.
How insulin lowers blood glucose
  • When blood glucose is too high, the pancreas releases insulin into the bloodstream.
  • Insulin binds to receptors on certain body cells.
  • This tells cells to take in glucose from the blood.
  • Liver cells and muscle cells are especially important because they take up extra glucose.
  • These cells convert glucose into glycogen, a storage form of glucose.
  • Because glucose is removed from the blood, the blood glucose concentration falls back toward normal.
How glucagon raises blood glucose
  • If blood glucose falls too low, for example after missing a meal, the pancreas releases glucagon.
  • Glucagon has the opposite effect to insulin.
  • It acts mainly on the liver.
  • Liver cells break down stored glycogen into glucose molecules.
  • These glucose molecules are then released into the bloodstream.
  • This causes blood glucose concentration to rise back toward normal.

Remember: glucagon is a hormone, while glycogen is a storage carbohydrate made from many glucose molecules.

Negative feedback control
  • Blood glucose is controlled by a negative feedback loop.
  • If glucose gets too high, insulin is released to lower it.
  • If glucose gets too low, glucagon is released to raise it.
  • The body constantly releases these hormones as needed to keep blood glucose close to the correct level.
  • This is why blood glucose level fluctuates slightly instead of staying completely flat.
