16. Gas Exchange in the Lungs

Key Notes (English)

Adapted from the provided speech. Focus: structure and function of the lungs and how alveoli are adapted for gas exchange.

1) Big Picture
  • The lungs move oxygen from air into the blood and remove carbon dioxide from the blood to the air.
  • Cells need oxygen to carry out aerobic respiration (to release energy). CO2 is a waste product of this process.
  • Gas exchange happens in the alveoli (singular: alveolus).
2) Airway Pathway
  • Air enters via nose or mouth → down the trachea (windpipe).
  • The trachea divides into two bronchi → repeatedly branch into bronchioles.
  • Bronchioles end in clusters of tiny air sacs: alveoli, like bunches of grapes.
3) Alveolar Adaptations for Rapid Diffusion
  • Thin barrier Alveolar wall and capillary wall are each one cell thick → very short diffusion distance.
  • Huge surface area Hundreds of millions of alveoli in adults → combined area ≈ about half a tennis court.
  • Moist lining Gases dissolve in the thin moisture layer → faster diffusion.
  • Rich blood supply & continuous flow Capillaries constantly bring deoxygenated blood and carry away oxygenated blood, maintaining steep concentration gradients.
4) What Diffuses and How It’s Carried
  • Oxygen: high in alveoli, low in incoming blood → diffuses into capillaries; then carried by haemoglobin in red blood cells.
  • Carbon dioxide: higher in blood than in alveoli → diffuses into alveoli and is exhaled. It is carried mainly dissolved in blood plasma (not bound to haemoglobin, in this context).
5) Breathing Rate Calculation
  • Breathing rate (breaths per minute) = number of breaths ÷ time (in minutes).
  • Example: 42 breaths in 3 minutes → 42 ÷ 3 = 14 breaths·min⁻¹.
  • During exercise your rate increases to deliver more O2 and remove more CO2.
