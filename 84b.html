84B. Trophic Levels

Key Notes (Synced with Video)

0:00 - 0:39 | Trophic Levels and Producers
  • Trophic levels are the different feeding levels in a food chain.
  • In a simple chain, grass is level 1, rabbit is level 2, snake is level 3, and hawk is level 4.
  • The first trophic level is always made up of producers, such as plants or algae.
  • Producers use photosynthesis to make their own food using energy from sunlight.
  • Sunlight captured by producers is the original energy source for the whole food chain.
0:44 - 1:20 | Primary and Secondary Consumers
  • The second trophic level contains primary consumers.
  • Primary consumers are the first organisms in the chain to eat other organisms.
  • They are usually herbivores because they feed on plants or algae.
  • The third trophic level contains secondary consumers.
  • Secondary consumers are carnivores because they eat other animals.
1:20 - 2:31 | Higher Consumers and Energy Transfer
  • Organisms in the fourth trophic level are tertiary consumers.
  • Tertiary consumers are usually carnivores that eat secondary consumers.
  • Most food chains have only four or five trophic levels.
  • Energy is lost each time it passes from one trophic level to the next.
  • Only about 10% of the energy in one trophic level is transferred to the next level.
  • For example, 1000 J at level 1 may become 100 J at level 2, 10 J at level 3, and 1 J at level 4.
2:31 - 3:04 | Apex Predators and Omnivores
  • Apex predators are carnivores at the top of a food chain with no predators of their own.
  • Omnivores, such as bears, eat both producers and other animals.
  • Omnivores do not fit perfectly into one trophic level.
  • They are often placed in trophic level 3 because they can eat primary consumers.
  • However, they may also feed directly on producers.
3:12 - 3:32 | Decomposers and Nutrient Recycling
  • Decomposers include bacteria, fungi, and small animals such as worms.
  • They break down dead plant and animal matter in the environment.
  • Decomposition recycles nutrients back into the soil.
  • These nutrients can then be used again by producers.
  • Decomposers are essential for keeping nutrients moving through ecosystems.
