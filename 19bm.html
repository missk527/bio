Notes (Exam-ready key points)

The four main components of blood
  • Red blood cells (RBCs): carry oxygen from the lungs to body tissues for cellular respiration.
  • White blood cells (WBCs): part of the immune system; defend against infection.
  • Platelets: fragments of cells that trigger clotting at wounds.
  • Plasma: pale, straw-coloured liquid that carries cells and many dissolved substances.
Red blood cells (structure & function)
  • Contain red pigment haemoglobin which binds O2 to form oxyhaemoglobin.
  • Oxyhaemoglobin releases O2 in tissues so oxygen can diffuse into cells.
  • No nucleus → more internal space for haemoglobin/oxygen.
  • Biconcave disc shape → large surface area for O2 absorption.
White blood cells (roles)
  • Make up < 1% of blood but are essential for immunity.
  • Phagocytosis: engulf and digest pathogens.
  • Antibody production: antibodies bind to pathogens and help destroy them.
  • Antitoxin production: neutralise toxins produced by pathogens.
  • Have a nucleus (unlike RBCs).
Platelets (clotting)
  • Not full cells — they are small cell fragments → no nucleus.
  • Form clots to seal wounds (“patch the hole”).
  • Clotting prevents blood loss and blocks microorganisms from entering.
Plasma (the transport medium)
  • Makes up just over half of blood volume; keeps blood liquid so it can flow.
  • Carries RBCs, WBCs, platelets.
  • Also carries: glucose, amino acids (nutrients); carbon dioxide, urea (wastes); hormones, proteins, and antibodies/antitoxins from WBCs.
Replacing lost blood
  • Adults typically have about ~5 litres of blood in circulation.
  • Severe loss (e.g., injury or surgery) can reduce oxygen delivery to tissues.
  • Artificial blood (saline volume expanders): adds volume so the heart can keep pumping, but contains no RBCs → no extra oxygen transport; practical limit ~ one‑third replacement.
  • Blood transfusion: donor blood with real RBCs; restores oxygen‑carrying capacity and is the better option for significant loss.
