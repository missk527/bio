Vaccines: Immunity, Antigens, Pros & Cons

Notes (rewritten in English from the speech)

Immunity and pathogens
  • After the immune system is exposed to a pathogen, it usually develops immunity to that pathogen and the disease it causes.
  • Once immune, you are very unlikely to catch the same disease again (e.g., many people only get chickenpox once).
  • The problem is that natural immunity often requires you to catch the disease first, which can be unpleasant or even deadly.
Pathogen Immunity Disease prevention
What vaccines do
  • Vaccines aim to give you immunity without you having to suffer the disease.
  • A vaccine contains the pathogen (or parts of it) in a form that is weakened or inactivated, so it cannot cause the disease.
  • Even when weakened/inactivated, it still has the same antigens, so the immune system responds.
Antigens and antibodies
  • Antigens are identifying molecules on a pathogen that the immune system recognises.
  • When antigens are detected, the body produces antibodies and develops immunity.
  • This prepares the immune system to respond quickly if the real pathogen appears later.
What vaccines protect against
  • Vaccines can work against both bacteria and viruses.
  • By the time children start primary school, they have often had vaccines against many diseases, some of which could otherwise be fatal.
Pros (benefits)
  • Main benefit: protection from catching serious diseases.
  • On a population level, vaccines helped control diseases that used to be common, such as polio, smallpox, measles, and whooping cough.
  • They also help prevent large outbreaks called epidemics, where disease spreads rapidly and can kill many people.
Herd immunity
  • If enough people are immune, a pathogen has few or no hosts to spread to.
  • The pathogen can then die out in the population once infected hosts recover or die.
  • This indirectly protects people who are not vaccinated, because they are surrounded by immune people.
Cons (limitations and risks)
  • Vaccines do not always work perfectly; sometimes they do not give full immunity.
  • Some people may have reactions (e.g., swelling at the injection site).
  • Severe reactions (e.g., fever or seizures) can happen but are rare, and very severe reactions are extremely rare.
  • Overall, the speech states the pros usually outweigh the cons.
