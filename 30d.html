Body Defences: Barriers & Immune System

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Overview of the body’s defence system
  • The human body has a defence system that protects us from pathogens (harmful microorganisms such as bacteria and viruses).
  • We can split this system into two main parts:
    Physical & chemical barriers Immune system
  • Physical and chemical barriers stop pathogens from getting into the body.
  • The immune system destroys any pathogens that manage to get inside.
2) Skin – a physical and chemical barrier
  • Most of our body is covered in skin, which acts as a tough physical barrier that pathogens cannot easily get through.
  • The skin also produces oils and antimicrobial substances.
Skin as a defence • Physical barrier: blocks entry of pathogens. • Chemical defence: secretes oils and antimicrobial substances. • These can kill or damage pathogens resting on the skin.
  • Because skin is so effective, pathogens often try to enter through holes in the body surface, such as the nose and mouth.
3) Nose, airways and stomach – trapping and destroying pathogens
  • The nose helps defend us by having:
    • lots of small hairs,
    • lots of sticky mucus.
  • These trap pathogens and particles in the air as we breathe.
Nose • Hairs and mucus trap pathogens and dust. • Reduces the number of pathogens reaching deeper parts of the body.
  • If pathogens get past the nose, they must go through either the trachea (to the lungs) or the oesophagus (to the stomach).
  • The trachea, bronchi and bronchioles are all coated in a layer of mucus which traps particles and pathogens.
Cilia in the airways • The airways are lined with cilia: - tiny hair-like structures on cells. • Cilia move in a rhythmic way. • They slowly move the mucus (with trapped pathogens) up to the back of the throat. • We then swallow the mucus into the stomach.
  • The stomach contains hydrochloric acid (HCl), a very strong acid.
Stomach acid • Brings stomach pH down to about 2. • This very acidic environment kills almost all pathogens in: - swallowed mucus - food we have eaten.
  • These defences together make it very hard for pathogens to survive and reach our internal tissues.
4) Immune system and white blood cells
  • Despite strong barriers, some pathogens still enter the body.
  • When this happens, the immune system takes over.
  • The most important cells in this system are white blood cells.
  • White blood cells:
    • continually circulate in the blood and tissues,
    • patrol the body looking for pathogens.
White blood cells – three key roles 1) Phagocytosis (engulfing pathogens) 2) Producing antitoxins 3) Producing antibodies
5) Phagocytosis & antitoxins
  • Phagocytosis is a process where certain white blood cells engulf and digest pathogens.
Phagocytosis • White blood cell tracks the pathogen. • Binds to the pathogen. • Engulfs it (surrounds it). • Destroys it inside the cell.
  • Some pathogens make toxins, which are tiny poisons that can damage our cells and make us feel ill.
  • White blood cells respond by producing antitoxins.
Antitoxins • Small molecules made by white blood cells. • Bind to the toxins produced by pathogens. • Neutralise (counteract) the toxins so they cannot harm our cells.
6) Antigens, antibodies and immunity
  • Pathogens have antigens on their surface.
Antigens • Substances that the immune system recognises as "foreign". • Examples: - parts of a bacterial cell wall, - proteins on the surface of a virus.
  • Our white blood cells produce antibodies.
Antibodies • Small proteins made by white blood cells. • Each type of antibody is specific to one particular antigen. • They "lock onto" that antigen. • Act as a signal to other white blood cells to come and destroy the pathogen.
  • Each antibody only fits one type of antigen, like a key in a lock.
  • Once the immune system has found an antibody that binds the pathogen, it starts making large numbers of that antibody.
  • The immune system also remembers which antibody works.
Immunity • If the same pathogen infects us again: - our body quickly makes many of the correct antibodies, - pathogens are destroyed before they can make us feel ill. • This is what it means to be "immune" to a disease.
