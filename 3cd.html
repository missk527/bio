Cell Differentiation & Stem Cells

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

Undifferentiated vs. Specialized Cells
  • Undifferentiated cells (e.g., stem cells) have not yet adopted a specific structure or function.
  • Specialized cells have distinct shapes, organelles, and roles (e.g., nerve cells, muscle cells, sperm, egg).
  • Multicellular organisms need many cell types—like a building needs many materials.
Start of Life & Gametes
  • In animals, life begins when a sperm cell fertilizes an egg cell.
  • Both gametes are specialized:
    • Sperm: small; delivers the father’s DNA; contains many mitochondria for tail movement.
    • Egg: large; stores nutrients to start development.
  • Each gamete carries half the usual DNA (haploid), so the zygote has the normal amount after fusion.
From Zygote to Stem Cells
  • After fertilization, the zygote divides into a cluster of identical cells.
  • These early cells are embryonic stem cells: fully undifferentiated and capable of becoming any cell type.
  • Differentiation = the process by which cells become specialized for specific functions.
Examples of Specialized Animal Cells
  • Muscle cells: elongated, packed with mitochondria to power contraction.
  • Nerve cells (neurons): adapted for rapid signaling (covered in more detail elsewhere).
  • Sperm cells: streamlined for motility and DNA delivery.

In fully developed animals, most cells are fixed in their roles and can no longer differentiate.

Adult Stem Cells
  • Some undifferentiated cells remain in adults—especially in the bone marrow.
  • Adult stem cells mainly produce different kinds of blood cells (limited potency).
  • They help replace lost or damaged cells (e.g., skin, blood).
Plants: Specialization & Meristems
  • Plants also have specialized cells:
    • Root hair cells: increase surface area for water and mineral uptake.
    • Phloem cells: transport sugars and other nutrients.
    • Xylem cells: transport water.
  • Meristems are growth regions that stay stem-cell-like throughout life (do not fully differentiate).
  • Small cuttings can be used to clone plants quickly and cheaply.
  • Applications: propagate rare species; mass-produce identical crops with desirable traits (e.g., large fruits).
Medical Uses of Stem Cells
  • Two key animal stem cell types:
    • Embryonic stem cells: can become any cell type.
    • Adult stem cells (bone marrow): limited to blood cell lineages.
  • In the lab, stem cells are cloned to produce many identical cells for therapy or research.
  • Existing therapy: transfer of bone marrow stem cells to replace faulty blood cells.
  • Potential future therapies (with embryonic stem cells): replace damaged neurons in paralysis; restore insulin-producing cells in diabetes; and many others.
Risks, Rejection & Therapeutic Cloning
  • Immune rejection: transplanted cells from another person may be recognized as foreign.
  • Therapeutic cloning: create an embryo with the patient’s DNA so derived stem cells are genetically matched and less likely to be rejected.
  • Infection risk: stem cells grown in the lab could acquire a virus and pass it to the patient.
Ethical & Legal Considerations
  • Some object to using human embryos due to their potential for life.
  • Embryos used in research are often surplus from fertility clinics that would otherwise be destroyed.
  • Regulation varies by country; in the UK, stem cell research is legal under strict criteria.
