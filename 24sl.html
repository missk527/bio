Levels of organization in plants
  • Plants, like animals, have hierarchical organization: similar cells → tissues → organs → organ systems.
  • The leaf is an organ. Together with the stem and roots, it forms an organ system for transporting substances around the plant.

Leaf function (why its structure matters)
  • Leaves are the site of photosynthesis (covered elsewhere).
  • They need a steady supply of carbon dioxide and water to make sugars.
  • Water comes from the soil via roots and is transported up by the xylem.
  • Carbon dioxide diffuses from the outside air into the leaf through small pores called stomata (plural of stoma).
Cross-section of a leaf (from bottom to top)
  • Lower epidermis: contains many stomata (tiny pores) scattered across this epidermal layer.
  • Spongy mesophyll: loosely packed cells with air spaces that allow CO₂ to diffuse through.
  • Palisade mesophyll: columnar cells packed with chloroplasts; this is where most photosynthesis occurs.
  • Upper epidermis: nearly transparent to let sunlight pass to the palisade layer below.
  • Vascular bundles: green tubes in diagrams — phloem carries sugars away from the leaf; xylem runs alongside and brings water up from roots.

Note: The speech’s misspellings (e.g., “tomata”, “misoil”, “xyllem”, “phe/phum”) are corrected to stomata, mesophyll, xylem, and phloem.

Water loss & adaptations
  • Main challenge: water loss from the leaf surfaces.
  • Waxy cuticle on the upper surface: thin, lipid-based, waterproof layer reducing evaporation.
  • Stomata allow CO₂ in but also let water vapor out, so they are kept open for the shortest necessary time.
  • Guard cells control each stoma:
    • Turgid (well hydrated): pore opens wider → more CO₂ diffusion.
    • Flaccid (water lost by osmosis): pore closes → conserve water.
    • Guard cells are light-sensitive: they close at night when photosynthesis is not occurring.
  • Most stomata are on the underside (cooler, more shaded) to reduce evaporation.
Growth tissue
  • Meristem tissue (plant stem cells) is found at the growing tips of roots and shoots.
  • Meristems can differentiate into many cell types, enabling growth.
