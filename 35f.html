Limiting Factors of Photosynthesis — Notes & Quiz Light · CO₂ · Temperature · Chlorophyll

Video + exam‑focused notes + 40‑question practice

Notes (Key points from the speech)

What controls the rate of photosynthesis?
  • Plants must photosynthesise to survive, but the rate depends on four main factors:
  • Light intensity, temperature, carbon dioxide (CO₂) concentration, and the amount of chlorophyll.
Chlorophyll
  • Chlorophyll is the pigment in chloroplasts that absorbs light energy for photosynthesis.
  • If a plant has less chlorophyll, it absorbs less light and the rate of photosynthesis falls.
  • Chlorophyll levels vary between plant species, and can also drop in one plant due to disease or stress.
  • Examples mentioned: infection by tobacco mosaic virus, environmental stress, or lack of resources (e.g. water / nutrients) that can damage chloroplasts.
Limiting factors (what we usually mean)
  • In exam questions, “limiting factors” usually refers to light, CO₂, and temperature.
  • On graphs: the factor goes on the x‑axis and rate of photosynthesis on the y‑axis.
Light intensity graph
  • As light intensity increases, the rate increases because light provides energy for photosynthesis.
  • Eventually the curve levels off (plateaus).
  • A plateau means another factor (often CO₂ or temperature) has become the limiting factor.
CO₂ concentration graph
  • CO₂ is a reactant in photosynthesis, so increasing CO₂ can increase the rate.
  • The curve can also plateau once CO₂ is no longer limiting.
Temperature graph
  • The rate rises at first as enzymes work faster.
  • After an optimum, the rate falls because enzymes begin to denature.
  • By about 45°C, enzymes may be fully denatured and the rate can fall to zero.
Comparing curves on one graph
  • Some graphs show two or more curves from different experiments.
  • Example: light intensity tested at 15°C vs 25°C. A higher maximum at 25°C suggests temperature limited the 15°C experiment.
  • Another example: same temperature but CO₂ is 10× higher for the top curve. The lower curve is limited by CO₂ concentration.
How farmers increase photosynthesis (greenhouses)
  • Farmers try to increase yield by creating conditions for a high photosynthesis rate.
  • Greenhouses trap heat, raising temperature.
  • Artificial light can extend photosynthesis beyond daylight hours.
  • CO₂ can be increased by pumping it in or using a paraffin heater (releases heat and CO₂).
  • Greenhouses reduce pests/pathogens; farmers may also use fertilisers (minerals) and pesticides (kill pests).
  • These methods cost money, so farmers weigh extra cost against extra yield.
