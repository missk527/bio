The Heart & Circulatory System

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

What the Circulatory System Does
  • The circulatory system is an organ system that transports oxygen and nutrients to tissues and carries wastes away.
  • It relies on three parts: blood (the transport medium), blood vessels (tubes), and the heart (the pump).
Double Circulation: Two Loops
  • Pulmonary loop: carries deoxygenated blood from the heart to the lungs, gains oxygen, then returns oxygenated blood to the heart.
  • Systemic loop: sends oxygenated blood from the heart to the body’s tissues, where oxygen is used, and returns deoxygenated blood to the heart.
Heart Structure & Orientation
  • The heart has four chambers: two atria at the top and two ventricles at the bottom, with thick muscular walls.
  • In most diagrams you view the heart as if facing a person: the heart’s left side appears on your right, and the right side on your left.
  • Valves between chambers and vessels prevent backflow and keep blood moving in one direction.
Path of Blood Through the Heart
  • Blood enters via the venae cavae (from the body) into the right atrium and via the pulmonary veins (from the lungs) into the left atrium.
  • Both atria contract together, pushing blood into the right and left ventricles.
  • Ventricles then contract: the right ventricle pumps into the pulmonary artery (to the lungs) and the left ventricle pumps into the aorta (to the body).
  • The cycle repeats continuously: ~70 beats per minute, over 100,000 beats per day.
Keeping the Beat: Pacemaker
  • A group of cells in the right atrium acts as a natural pacemaker, generating electrical impulses that spread through the heart muscle and trigger contraction.
  • If these cells malfunction, doctors can implant an artificial pacemaker under the skin with a lead to the heart to maintain a regular rhythm.
Arteries vs. Veins (and the Pulmonary Exception)
  • Artery: any vessel that carries blood away from the heart.
  • Vein: any vessel that carries blood toward the heart.
  • Most arteries carry oxygenated blood and most veins carry deoxygenated blood, but there are exceptions:
  • The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood to the lungs (still an artery because it goes away from the heart).
  • The pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood to the heart (veins because they return to the heart).
The Heart’s Own Blood Supply
  • The heart muscle needs oxygen and nutrients too.
  • Coronary arteries, small branches from the aorta, encircle the heart and supply the cardiac muscle.
