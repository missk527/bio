Notes (Key points rewritten from the speech)

Why organisms must exchange with their environment
  • To take in useful substrates (e.g., nutrients, oxygen) and remove waste (e.g., carbon dioxide).
  • This exchange helps maintain a stable internal environment (homeostasis) — inputs and outputs must be finely balanced.
Single‑celled vs. multicellular organisms
  • Single‑celled organisms (e.g., bacteria) exchange substances directly across the cell surface.
  • They are very small, so they have a large surface‑area‑to‑volume (SA:V) ratio, allowing efficient diffusion of the few substances they need.
  • Example: during respiration, O2 diffuses in from air and CO2 diffuses out to the atmosphere.
  • Very small multicellular organisms may still rely on diffusion alone, but this becomes inadequate as size increases.
SA:V ratio and size — mouse vs. elephant cubes
Model the mouse as a 1 cm cube and the elephant as a 10 cm cube:
  • Mouse cube: surface area = 6 cm²; volume = 1 cm³ → SA:V = 6:1.
  • Elephant cube: surface area = 600 cm²; volume = 1000 cm³ → SA:V ≈ 0.6:1.

Conclusion: as organisms get bigger, their SA:V ratio falls, so diffusion alone becomes too slow to supply all cells.

Why large organisms need specialised exchange systems
  • Lower SA:V and increased diffusion distances mean simple diffusion is too slow to meet metabolic demands.
  • Therefore, most multicellular organisms have specialised structures to exchange materials with the environment.
  • Examples: alveoli (lungs), villi (small intestine), leaf surfaces & root hair cells (plants), and gills (fish).
Four common features of effective exchange surfaces
  • Large surface area relative to volume → more particles can cross at once.
  • Very thin barriers → short diffusion distance and faster diffusion.
  • Permeable membranes to the substance being exchanged.
  • A maintained concentration (diffusion) gradient. Often created by neighbouring systems — e.g., lungs supply O2 while the bloodstream continuously removes O2, keeping the gradient steep.
Core definitions for the exam
  • Diffusion: net movement of particles from higher to lower concentration.
  • Homeostasis: maintenance of a stable internal environment.
  • SA:V ratio: surface area divided by volume; typically decreases as size increases.
