DNA Structure

Notes

  • DNA is made of two strands twisted together in a shape called a double helix.
  • Each DNA strand is a polymer made from many repeating monomers called nucleotides.
  • Every nucleotide has three parts: a phosphate group, a sugar, and a base.
  • The phosphate and sugar are the same in every nucleotide.
  • The base can vary, giving four DNA bases: adenine (A), thymine (T), cytosine (C), and guanine (G).
  • Because there are four possible bases, there are four types of DNA nucleotide.
  • Nucleotides join when the phosphate of one bonds to the sugar of the next.
  • This repeating pattern forms the sugar-phosphate backbone of each DNA strand.
  • The sugar-phosphate backbone makes the outer part of the DNA molecule.
  • The bases point inward from the backbone and help hold the two strands together.
  • The second DNA strand runs in the opposite direction to the first strand.
  • Bases pair by complementary base pairing.
  • A always pairs with T, and C always pairs with G.
  • Complementary base pairing lets us work out the matching sequence on the opposite strand.
  • The genetic code means the sequence of bases in DNA.
  • A gene is a specific sequence of bases that codes for a particular protein.
  • The base sequence is read in groups of three called triplets.
  • Each triplet codes for one specific amino acid.
  • The amino acids are joined together in the same order as the triplets.
  • The long amino acid chain then folds into a protein.
  • Different proteins have different amino acid sequences and therefore different shapes.
  • A protein’s shape helps it carry out a particular function.
  • Proteins are important as enzymes, hormones, and structural proteins.
