Plant Transport: Translocation & Transpiration

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

Big picture
  • Plants move two main things: sugars (via translocation in the phloem) and water + mineral ions (via transpiration in the xylem).
  • Photosynthesis happens mostly in the leaves, so sugars must be distributed to non‑photosynthetic parts.
Phloem & translocation (sugars)
  • Phloem consists of columns of living sieve tube elements arranged end to end (with many pores between adjacent cells).
  • The pores allow movement of cell sap — a mixture of water and dissolved sugars.
  • Sugars made in leaves can be transported long distances to other organs.
  • Arriving sugars are used for energy or stored for later use.
  • Phloem transport is bidirectional (up or down the plant), depending on source and sink.
Xylem & transpiration (water + minerals)
  • Xylem are columns of dead cells with no end walls, forming continuous hollow tubes.
  • Walls are strengthened with lignin.
  • Xylem carries water and mineral ions upward from roots → stem → leaves.
What drives water movement?
  • Water movement is driven by evaporation from leaf surfaces through stomata.
  • Think of water molecules as a continuous chain: when some evaporate, they pull the next molecules up — the transpiration stream.
Transpiration rate: 4 key factors
  • Light intensity: More light → more photosynthesis → stomata open → higher evaporation → higher transpiration. At night, stomata close → very low transpiration.
  • Temperature: Warmer air gives water molecules more energy → they evaporate and diffuse out faster → higher transpiration.
  • Air flow (wind): Moving air removes moist air near the leaf → keeps the outside concentration low → steeper gradient → higher transpiration.
  • Humidity: More humid air reduces the inside–outside concentration gradient → lower transpiration.
Diffusion gradient inside the leaf
  • Inside the leaf is kept moist because water is continually delivered from roots.
  • Since outside air is usually drier, water diffuses out down its concentration gradient; the strength of this gradient controls the rate.
